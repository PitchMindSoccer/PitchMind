## 1. POSITION OVERVIEW

### What is a Goalkeeper's Distribution Role?

The modern goalkeeper operates in three distinct phases:

1. **Shot-Stopping Phase (Defensive):** Preventing goals through saves, positioning, command of the box
2. **Distribution Phase (Possession/Buildup):** Playing the ball from the goalkeeper's hands into play, initiating attacks
3. **Sweeper-Keeper Phase (Advanced):** Operating outside the penalty area as a quasi-midfielder or 3rd center-back

Historically, distribution was secondary. Today, at elite levels, distribution has become almost co-equal with shot-stopping as a critical skill. The best goalkeepers excel at both.

### Three Modern GK Archetypes

#### The Controller (Ederson, Modern Neuer, Ter Stegen)
- Primary characteristic: High technical ability, comfortable receiving and distributing in all areas
- Tactical role: Initiates possession sequences, often plays to feet from 25+ yards away
- Risk profile: Conservative but creative. Makes high-percentage plays but occasionally attempts complex distributions
- Physical profile: Often technically gifted, good with both feet, lower height (6'2"-6'3") but excellent positioning

#### The Directioner (Leno, Sommer, Ramsdale)
- Primary characteristic: Balanced distribution approach, uses both short and long passes intelligently
- Tactical role: Reads opposition pressing and responds with appropriate ball progression
- Risk profile: Moderate. Willing to take calculated risks to break pressing or spring counter-attacks
- Physical profile: Athletic, comfortable on the ball, good kicking range (50+ yard passes)

#### The Safety-First Player (Courtois, Oblak in defensive phases, Alisson in structured teams)
- Primary characteristic: Conservative distribution, prioritizes accuracy over creativity
- Tactical role: Protects lead, prevents turnovers in dangerous areas
- Risk profile: Low. Prefers 95% pass completion over 80% with creative attempts
- Physical profile: Exceptionally strong shot-stopping, may be less comfortable in 1v1 distribution duels

## 2. DISTRIBUTION DECISION-MAKING FRAMEWORK

### Pre-Reception Assessment (1-2 seconds before receiving the ball)

When a goalkeeper receives a backpass or a deliberate pass from a defender, the goalkeeper should conduct a rapid assessment:

| Factor | Questions to Ask | Decision Impact |
|--------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Pressing structure** | How many opposition players are within 15 yards? Are they coordinated or isolated? | 1-2 players = likely can play short; 3+ players = consider long or sweep |
| **Space availability** | Where is free space? Midfield lane? Wide area? Forward-facing space? | Direct your distribution to the space |
| **Receiver quality** | What's my first pass option's technical level? Can they receive under pressure? | Choose receiver based on their comfort level, not just availability |
| **Game state** | What's the score? How much time? Opposition pressing style? | 2-0 up = risk-averse; 0-1 down = risk-accepting |
| **Possession level** | Do we have 65% possession or 35%? | High possession = build patiently; low possession = consider directness |

### First Touch Orientation

The goalkeeper's first touch is a signal to themselves and the opposition about their intention:

- **Touch away from pressure:** Goalkeeper receives with a touch that creates space. This suggests they're looking to distribute or hold for a teammate's movement.
- **Touch lateral (sideways):** Goalkeeper receives laterally, buying time. This suggests uncertainty or waiting for a new passing option.
- **Touch toward pressure:** Goalkeeper receives with a touch into the pressing player. This is a mistake — it suggests poor decision-making or panic.

**Elite goalkeepers** consistently orient their first touch away from pressure and toward their intended distribution space. This is coachable.

### Distribution Pass Hierarchy

Ranked by safety and field position progression:

#### Tier 1: Ultra-Safe Passes (95%+ success rate)
- Short pass to center-back or fullback, <15 yards, with space
- Receiver is unmarked and has clear field ahead
- Execution: Roll pass or short kick
- Outcome: Ball is secure in defender's possession, minimal counter-attack risk

#### Tier 2: Safe Distribution (85-92% success rate)
- Pass to midfielder, 20-30 yards, with moderate space
- Receiver has positioning advantage but might face light pressure
- Execution: Ground pass or short chip
- Outcome: Ball progresses to midfield, team retains possession

#### Tier 3: Calculated Risk (70-85% success rate)
- Long pass, 35-50 yards, to bypass pressing
- Receiver is in advanced area, might face immediate pressure
- Execution: Long diagonal, switch play, or chip
- Outcome: If successful, significant field progression. If unsuccessful, turnover in midfield or advanced area

#### Tier 4: High Risk (50-70% success rate)
- Very long pass, 50+ yards
- Receiver is in attacking third or advanced area
- Execution: Direct, driven pass or high pass
- Outcome: If successful, potential for goal-scoring opportunity. If unsuccessful, dangerous counter-attack or immediate shot

#### Tier 5: Rejection Pass (Variable success, <50% in some contexts)
- Clearance, punt, or thrown out pass
- Goalkeeper is giving up possession
- Execution: Kick or throw
- Outcome: Possession is lost, but goalkeeper is out of immediate danger

## 3. RECEIVING UNDER PRESSURE — DECISION TREE

### High-Pressure Scenarios (3+ opposition players pressing)

```
Receive pass in goal area with 3+ opposition pressing
|
├─ OPTION A: Immediate short pass to nearest defender
│  └─ Best if: Defender is clearly open, pass is 10 yards or less, opposition press is uncoordinated
│  └─ Risk: If misplayed, turnover in your own box
│
├─ OPTION B: Hold the ball 2-3 seconds, let pressing player overshoot
│  └─ Best if: You have strong first touch, defender is on a tight angle
│  └─ Risk: If keeper loses composure, next pass is panicked
│
├─ OPTION C: Play long (40+ yard pass)
│  └─ Best if: You want to bypass press entirely, forward run is available, or you're losing late
│  └─ Risk: If pass is inaccurate, turnover in dangerous area
│
└─ OPTION D: Clearance/Kick (give up possession safely)
   └─ Best if: All passing options are marked, threat is immediate
   └─ Risk: Possession loss, but goalkeeper is safe
```

### Moderate-Pressure Scenarios (1-2 opposition players pressing)

In this situation, most elite goalkeepers attempt short passing. The press is tight enough to require a quick decision, but loose enough that there should be an open passing lane.

**Key skill:** Reading where the open lane is. If opposition is pressing on the left, the open lane is likely right-sided. Scan for it pre-reception.

### Low-Pressure Scenarios (0 opposition players actively pressing)

Goalkeeper has time. Choices expand significantly:

- **Option A:** Play patiently. Take 2-3 seconds, allow midfielders to move into new positions, then pass into space
- **Option B:** Play quickly. Rapid 1-2 touch pass to set tempo and rhythm
- **Option C:** Play creatively. Attempt more complex distributions (chip pass, diagonal switch) that require higher technical quality but can unlock defenses

## 4. SWEEPER-KEEPER POSITIONING & DECISION-MAKING

### When Should a Goalkeeper Sweep?

#### Scenario 1: Pressure Relief (Defender Under Direct Pressure)

**Situation:** Your fullback is receiving a pass with an opposition attacker tight behind them.

**Goalkeeper's decision:**
- Can I reach the area (15-20 yards out) in time to provide an outlet?
- If I step out, am I creating a 1v1 vulnerability, or am I actually reducing danger?
- Can my defenders cover the space I'm leaving in the goal?

**Elite response:** Most modern goalkeepers step out in this situation because:
1. It creates a 3v2 (or better) for your team by providing a passing outlet
2. The defender is already in danger; adding a goalkeeper outlet actually reduces risk
3. If the goalkeeper misjudges and the attacker gets past both the fullback and goalkeeper, you're in trouble — but this is lower probability than the fullback being one-on-one

**Execution:** Goalkeeper moves decisively (not tentatively), positions themselves to receive a pass or block a pass forward, and prepares to either:
- Receive a pass and quickly play it forward or lateral
- Slide in to block a pass or shot if the fullback is beaten
- Return to goal if the danger passes

---

#### Scenario 2: Anticipatory Sweep (Reading an Opening Gap)

**Situation:** Offside line is tight, fullback has advanced without a cover shadow, or opposition is building an attack down your side.

**Goalkeeper's decision:**
- Is a gap actually developing, or am I overreacting?
- Can I reach the gap before the attacker?
- If I move and I'm wrong, how exposed is my goal?

**Elite response:** Advanced goalkeepers (Neuer, Ederson) read these situations 2-3 seconds in advance and move preventatively.

**Execution:** Goalkeeper:
1. Identifies the developing threat (attacker running into space)
2. Reads the fullback's shape (are they too high? Are they unable to cover?)
3. Makes a decision: advance to cover the gap or trust the fullback
4. If advancing, positions themselves to either intercept a pass or be available for a clearance

**Age and skill dependency:** This requires exceptional spatial reading. U10-U12 goalkeepers rarely execute this correctly because it requires anticipatory thinking 2-3 seconds in advance. This is an elite skill.

---

#### Scenario 3: Offensive Sweep (Quasi-Midfield Play)

**Situation:** Your team has secure possession (65%+ possession), opposition is retreating, and you're in a non-urgent phase of play.

**Goalkeeper's decision:**
- Is there a tactical advantage to moving up into midfield?
- Do I actually need an extra outlet, or are my defenders and midfielders already handling possession?

**Elite response:** Guardiola's teams occasionally use this, but sparingly. It's high-risk (turnover in midfield means a counter-attack) for moderate reward (an extra passing option).

**Execution:** Goalkeeper:
1. After playing a pass to a defender, moves 10-15 yards upfield
2. Becomes available for a third pass in a sequence
3. Quickly retreats if opposition wins the ball or if possession is lost

---

### Sweeper-Keeper Positioning Principles

| Principle | Implementation |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Stay compact** | Don't drift more than 20-25 yards from goal. If you're 30+ yards out, you're too far to recover if possession is lost. |
| **Angle of approach** | Approach attacks from a lateral angle (not directly at the attacker). This preserves goal line coverage and allows for better read of the play. |
| **Footwork** | Use small, quick steps when moving laterally. This allows for rapid redirection if the play shifts. |
| **Head on a swivel** | Constant scanning. Before moving, check: where's the ball? Where's the main attacking threat? Where's my nearest defender? |
| **Communication** | Call constantly. "Away!" if you're retreating, "You!" if you're taking the ball, "Stay!" if defenders should hold shape. |
| **Decisive commitment** | Once you decide to sweep, commit fully. Hesitation creates confusion and vulnerability. |

## 5. BUILDING OUT FROM THE BACK — COMMON PATTERNS

### Pattern 1: The Center-Back Rotation

**Scenario:** Goalkeeper plays to left center-back, who is unmarked. 

**Left CB's options:**
1. Play back to goalkeeper (risky, almost always wrong)
2. Play to right center-back (possession-secure but doesn't progress play)
3. Play to fullback (depends on marking)
4. Play to central midfielder (depends on pressing)

**Goalkeeper's role:** After playing to the left CB, position for a potential second pass. If the left CB faces pressure, you're the safety valve.

---

### Pattern 2: The Fullback Switch

**Scenario:** Goalkeeper plays short to fullback, who is marked. Fullback has no good forward option.

**Optimal progression:** Fullback passes back or lateral to center-back or goalkeeper. Goalkeeper then plays to the opposite fullback, bypassing the pressured zone.

**Requires:** Communication and quick passing rhythm. If any pass is slow, the press will reorganize and the switch becomes dangerous.

---

### Pattern 3: The Long Ball Bypass

**Scenario:** Your team's midfield is being pressed aggressively. Short passing options are limited.

**Goalkeeper's decision:** Play a long (40-50 yard) diagonal pass over the press to a forward or advancing fullback on the opposite side.

**Success requirements:**
1. Accurate pass (65-75% is acceptable for long passes; less is too high risk)
2. Forward/fullback is in space and ready to receive
3. Timing is correct (ball arrives at same time as player, not slow and interceptable)

## 6. AGE-GROUP CONSIDERATIONS FOR GK DISTRIBUTION

### U6-U10: Introduction Phase

**What to teach:**
- Basic passing mechanics (accuracy, weight, both feet)
- Reading simple spatial concepts (defender is open, midfielder is available)
- Simple distribution patterns (pass to closest open player)

**What NOT to overemphasize:**
- Sweeper-keeper activity (too advanced cognitively)
- Complex press reading (children lack the anticipatory thinking)
- Long-pass accuracy (focus on short passing foundation)

**Leo's scoring note:** At U6-U10, goalkeepers attempting complex distribution (long passes, sweeping) should be viewed with caution. It often indicates showboating rather than tactical understanding.

---

### U12-U14: Development Phase

**What to teach:**
- Intermediate distribution decisions (short vs. long, when to sweep)
- Reading moderate pressure (1-2 opposition players pressing)
- Introduction to anticipatory sweeping

**What to monitor:**
- Overconfidence. Some goalkeepers start attempting risky passes and become error-prone.
- Physical inconsistency. A 5'8" U12 goalkeeper has different kicking range than a 6'2" U12 goalkeeper.

**Leo's scoring note:** At U12-U14, inconsistency is expected. A goalkeeper who makes 5 good distribution choices and 2 risky mistakes is performing at age level. No penalty for the mistakes; credit for the attempts.

---

### U16-U18: Advanced Phase

**What to teach:**
- Complex press reading (3+ opposition players, coordinated vs. uncoordinated)
- Advanced sweeper-keeper timing (anticipatory, not just reactive)
- Game-state decision-making (risk varies by score, time, possession)

**What to expect:**
- High consistency (80%+ pass completion on all pass types)
- Active decision-making (not defaulting to safe passes)
- Occasional advanced sweeper-keeper plays

**Leo's scoring note:** At U16-U18, Leo should reward complex distribution attempts with high success. Penalize risky attempts that fail (turnover in dangerous area).

---

### College / Professional

**Expected standard:**
- 85%+ pass completion overall
- Active sweeper-keeper role (2-5 times per match in possession-heavy teams; 0-2 times in defensive-minded teams)
- Context-appropriate risk-taking

## 7. COMMON MISTAKES & ELITE CORRECTIONS

### Mistake 1: Panic Pass Under Pressure

**What it looks like:** Goalkeeper receives a backpass with 2-3 opposition players pressing, loses composure, plays a rushed pass directly into an opposition player's feet, causing an immediate turnover in the box.

**Why it happens:** 
- Poor pre-reception assessment (didn't see the press coming)
- Weak first touch (suggests the goalkeeper was surprised by the pass)
- Lack of confidence in distribution ability

**Elite correction:**
1. During training, practice receiving with coordinated pressing. The goalkeeper should experience 50 reps of "receive with 2-3 pressing" to build confidence.
2. Emphasize first-touch orientation. If you receive with your first touch away from pressure, you instantly have more time.
3. Practice "safe escape routes." Every goalkeeper should have a default safe pass (usually to the goalkeeper's keeper-side, away from the press).

---

### Mistake 2: Oversweeping

**What it looks like:** Goalkeeper steps out of the box prematurely or too far, leaving the goal exposed. Attacker chips over the goalkeeper or plays a quick pass into the space.

**Why it happens:**
- Overconfidence in reading the play
- Poor communication with defenders (they don't know you're sweeping)
- Lack of goal-line awareness

**Elite correction:**
1. Set a sweeping boundary. Goalkeepers should rarely sweep beyond 20-25 yards from goal. This preserves the recovery distance if possession is lost.
2. Require communication. Before sweeping, call it: "I'm going!" This allows defenders to adjust.
3. Drills with attackers. Practice sweeping against attackers who are trying to chip you. This builds realistic awareness.

---

### Mistake 3: Long-Pass Inaccuracy

**What it looks like:** Goalkeeper attempts a 50-yard pass, ball is misplaced (10-15 yards off target), opposition intercepts in midfield, dangerous counter-attack results.

**Why it happens:**
- Weak kicking technique (lack of power/accuracy)
- Poor read of receiver positioning (pass is aimed at wrong person)
- Environmental factors (wind, pitch conditions)

**Elite correction:**
1. Kicking drills. Target practice with long passes, aiming for specific zones (winger's feet, forward's chest, fullback's outside foot).
2. Study receiver positioning. Before the pass, visually confirm the receiver's position and movement pattern.
3. Adjust for environment. On a windy day, a 50-yard pass might become a 45-yard pass due to wind pushing the ball.

---

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Pressing Patterns

**What it looks like:** Opposition always presses your goalkeeper with the left midfielder + left fullback, creating a 2v1 on the left side. Goalkeeper repeatedly plays left-sided passes directly into the press, causing turnovers.

**Why it happens:**
- Lack of video review. Goalkeeper hasn't studied opposition patterns.
- Predictable distribution. Goalkeeper defaults to nearest open player without reading the press structure.

**Elite correction:**
1. Video analysis. Before matches, show the goalkeeper how opposition presses. "Their left mid always tucks inside. This creates a wide space on the right side."
2. Patterned training. Practice receiving passes with a specific opposition press structure. The goalkeeper learns to automatically play away from the press.

---

### Mistake 5: Risky Pass in Wrong Game State

**What it looks like:** Goalkeeper is 1-0 up with 15 minutes left, attempts a risky 45-yard pass to spring a counter-attack. Pass is intercepted, opposition gets a free shot. 1-1.

**Why it happens:**
- Lack of game state awareness. Goalkeeper doesn't adjust risk based on score/time.
- Coaching signal mismatch. Coach is asking for "aggressive distribution," but the game state calls for caution.

**Elite correction:**
1. Pre-match briefing. Coach explicitly states: "If we're up 1-0 after minute 70, prioritize possession over progression. Safe passes, not long balls."
2. Game state training. Practice the same receiving scenario with different scores/times, showing how the decision changes.

---

### Mistake 6: Poor Communication During Sweeping

**What it looks like:** Goalkeeper sweeps out of the box without calling it. Center-back doesn't see the goalkeeper moving and fills the space the goalkeeper just vacated. A 1v1 develops that shouldn't have.

**Why it happens:**
- Quiet goalkeeper. Goalkeepers who don't communicate constantly create confusion.
- Center-back is not scanning. Center-back should always know where the goalkeeper is.

**Elite correction:**
1. Communication drills. Goalkeeper and center-back practice together, with the goalkeeper calling movement: "Away!" (retreating), "You!" (taking the ball), "Stay!" (hold position).
2. Positioning clarity. If the goalkeeper sweeps, the center-back should shift to cover the goal line.

---

### Mistake 7: Ignoring Ball Speed Changes

**What it looks like:** Ball arrives to the goalkeeper slowly (soft back pass), goalkeeper is expecting a driven pass. First touch is too weak or mispositioned because the goalkeeper wasn't ready for the slow pace.

**Why it happens:**
- Lack of focus on the arriving pass. Goalkeeper is reading the press but not watching the ball closely.

**Elite correction:**
1. Constant scanning of the incoming pass. Watch the ball from the moment a teammate touches it.
2. Variable-pace drills. Practice receiving both soft and driven passes. Train the first touch to adapt.

---

### Mistake 8: Inaccurate Distance Assessment

**What it looks like:** Goalkeeper thinks they can reach a loose ball or an attacker's shot, attempts to sweep out aggressively, miscalculates the distance, and the attacker reaches the ball first or plays a pass into the space.

**Why it happens:**
- Poor spatial judgment at match speed
- Overconfidence in speed or reach
- Lack of repetition in game-realistic scenarios

**Elite correction:**
1. Reactive sweeping drills. Practice scenarios where the goalkeeper must quickly judge distance and decide: rush out or stay in goal?
2. Speed training. Goalkeepers who are faster can take more calculated risks. Build fitness to improve reaction time.

## 8. ELITE GOALKEEPER DISTRIBUTION PROFILES

### Ederson (Manchester City)

**Distribution signature:**
- Pass completion: 85%+ overall, 80%+ on passes over 30 yards
- Short-pass percentage: 70-75%
- Long-pass percentage: 25-30%
- Sweeper-keeper frequency: 2-3 times per match
- Typical distribution pattern: Receives backpass → 2-3 second pause → plays short pass or long diagonal

**Why this works in Pep's system:**
- Manchester City's high possession (62-65%) means short passes are always available
- Opposition presses Ederson, but Ederson's elite first touch and scanning creates passing lanes
- Long passes are used sparingly, usually to break a coordinated press

**Distinguishing feature:** Ederson appears calm under pressure. He takes full seconds to assess before passing, which is rare in modern soccer. This calm creates the impression of safety and security.

---

### Manuel Neuer (Bayern Munich, 2011-2015)

**Distribution signature:**
- Pass completion: 88%+ overall
- Short-pass percentage: 65-70%
- Long-pass percentage: 30-35%
- Sweeper-keeper frequency: 3-5 times per match (actively sweeping more than Ederson)
- Typical distribution pattern: Active sweeping to create passing lanes, frequent long diagonals to fullbacks/wingers

**Why this works:**
- Bayern's midfield press often creates space for the goalkeeper. Neuer reads this and plays into it.
- Long passes to fullbacks/wingers are part of Bayern's tactical identity.
- Neuer's exceptional speed and athleticism allow more aggressive sweeping.

**Distinguishing feature:** Neuer is proactive, not reactive. He moves before the ball, anticipating where it will go. This is why his sweeping appears so confident.

---

### Thibaut Courtois (Real Madrid)

**Distribution signature:**
- Pass completion: 85%+ overall, 75-80% on longer passes
- Short-pass percentage: 75-80%
- Long-pass percentage: 20-25%
- Sweeper-keeper frequency: 1-2 times per match (minimal)
- Typical distribution pattern: Conservative, preferring short passes. Occasional long passes when Real Madrid is counter-attacking.

**Why this works:**
- Real Madrid's defensive structure means the goalkeeper doesn't need to sweep frequently
- Courtois's elite shot-stopping is prioritized over distribution creativity
- Long passes are used strategically (not frequently) to spring Mbappé or Vinícius on the counter

**Distinguishing feature:** Courtois is effective without being "flashy." His distribution is reliable and mistake-free rather than creative.

## 9. DISTRIBUTION RADAR AXES FOR GK SCORING

Leo's system should assess goalkeepers on these dimensions:

### Axis 1: Distribution Accuracy (Execution)

**What to measure:** Successful pass percentage across all pass types and distances.

**Scoring rubric:**
- 85%+ pass completion overall: Excellent (9-10)
- 80-85% pass completion: Very Good (7-8)
- 75-80% pass completion: Good (6-7)
- 70-75% pass completion: Acceptable (5-6)
- Below 70%: Concerning (1-4)

**Context adjustments:**
- Age factor: U10-U12 goalkeepers should be held to 70-75% standard; U16-U18 to 80%+ standard
- Possession factor: Higher possession team (65%+) allows for higher accuracy standard. Lower possession (35-45%) may require lower standard due to more difficult passes.

---

### Axis 2: Risk Assessment & Decision-Making Under Pressure (Probability/IQ)

**What to measure:** Does the goalkeeper correctly assess when to attempt risky passes vs. safe ones?

**Scoring rubric:**
- Correct decision-making (risky pass when appropriate, safe pass when appropriate): 9-10
- Mostly correct (occasional misjudgment): 7-8
- Inconsistent decision-making: 5-6
- Frequently makes wrong decision (too risky or too conservative for context): 3-4
- Consistently poor decisions: 1-2

**Context assessment:**
- Is the goalkeeper attempting risky passes in 0-0 matches or when 1-0 down? (Good, high-variance decision-making)
- Is the goalkeeper refusing any risk when 2-0 down with 15 minutes? (Poor, context-ignoring)
- Is the goalkeeper assessing the opposition press correctly? (Attempting long passes when 3+ players are pressing vs. attempting short passes when opposition is deep)

---

### Axis 3: Sweeper-Keeper Timing & Positioning (Execution + Probability)

**What to measure:** When the goalkeeper sweeps, how accurate is their timing and positioning?

**Scoring rubric:**
- Perfect timing and positioning (reaches the ball/space before attacker, no miscommunication with defenders): 9-10
- Good timing, minor positioning issue (reaches ball but positioning isn't ideal): 7-8
- Acceptable (sweeps but it's somewhat reactive rather than anticipatory): 5-6
- Inconsistent sweeping (some good attempts, some dangerous misjudgments): 3-4
- Consistently poor sweeping (overextends, dangerous): 1-2

**Context adjustments:**
- Sweeping frequency should be age-adjusted. A U10 goalkeeper attempting one sweep per match is advanced; a professional goalkeeper should be attempting 2-5 per match (depending on system).

---

### Axis 4: Build-Up Pattern Recognition (Probability/IQ)

**What to measure:** Does the goalkeeper recognize team's preferred build-up patterns and execute them correctly?

**Scoring rubric:**
- Recognizes and executes patterns correctly: 9-10
- Mostly executes, occasional confusion: 7-8
- Inconsistent execution: 5-6
- Frequently breaks pattern or doesn't recognize it: 3-4
- No recognition of patterns: 1-2

**Patterns to assess:**
- Center-back rotation (does the goalkeeper position correctly for the second pass?)
- Fullback switch (does the goalkeeper understand the timing?)
- Long-ball bypass (does the goalkeeper know when opposition is pressing and execute the long pass?)

---

### Axis 5: Pressing Read & Pre-Pass Assessment (Probability/IQ)

**What to measure:** Does the goalkeeper correctly read opposition press before receiving the ball?

**Scoring rubric:**
- Excellent pre-pass awareness (adjusts body position, scans for press, identifies passing lanes before receiving): 9-10
- Good awareness (mostly correct, occasional misread): 7-8
- Acceptable (reads basic press structure but misses subtle patterns): 5-6
- Inconsistent (sometimes aware, sometimes caught off-guard): 3-4
- Poor awareness (frequently surprised by press): 1-2
