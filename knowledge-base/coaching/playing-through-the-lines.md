# Playing Through the Lines: Build-Up From Back to Attack

## What it is
"Playing through the lines" is a **systematic approach to progressing the ball from the defensive third through the midfield to the attacking third**, using structured passing sequences that maintain possession and gradually break opposition defensive lines.

Key difference from line-breaking:
- **Line-breaking**: Single pass that skips a defensive structure (direct, one-pass solution)
- **Playing through lines**: Multi-pass sequence (3-6 passes) that progressively advances through the opposition structure

Key components:
- **Structured build-up** from defenders
- **Support angles** and positioning for each pass
- **Third-man runs** to break lines progressively
- **Penetrating passes** at each stage
- **Timing** of each progressive pass

Playing through the lines is about **controlled progression**, not direct attacking.

## Why it matters (player development)
Modern soccer requires teams to play out from the back and build attacks systematically. Teams that play through lines:
- Win possession in advanced positions (not turnovers in dangerous areas)
- Maintain possession under pressure (controlled, not panicked)
- Create numerical advantages in midfield (more passes = more space created)
- Score from buildup (not just transitions)

Young players who master playing through lines:
- **Elite players**: Can progress through 3+ lines while under pressure; 80%+ success rate
- **Good players**: Can progress through midfield with coaching; 60-70% success
- **Developing players**: Struggle with sequencing; 40-50% success

This separates teams that control games from teams that are reactive.

## Universal principles (applies to ALL positions)

### Principle 1: The Three Phases of Playing Through Lines
Playing through lines happens in distinct phases, each with different objectives.

**Phase 1: Build-Up (Defensive Third)**
- **Objective**: Establish possession safely, start progression
- **Players involved**: Goalkeeper + 3-4 defenders (CB, FB, sometimes DM)
- **Passing pattern**: Short, lateral, safe passes to establish first foundation
- **Duration**: 3-5 passes typically
- **Key principle**: Safety first (don't lose ball in defensive area)

**Example sequence:**
```
GK → CB → FB → CB (lateral)
→ DM (move forward) → FB (support)
= 5 passes; now in deeper midfield
```

**Phase 2: Progression (Midfield Third)**
- **Objective**: Move ball forward through midfield; break first opposition line
- **Players involved**: Defensive mids + box-to-box mids + fullbacks
- **Passing pattern**: Longer passes, forward movement, third-man runs
- **Duration**: 3-4 passes typically
- **Key principle**: Break opposition's first line (middle field); create space for next phase

**Example sequence:**
```
DM → CB (receive back) 
→ DM (new position) → AM (forward)
→ AM (receive) = progressed through midfield
```

**Phase 3: Attack (Attacking Third)**
- **Objective**: Create clear chance near goal
- **Players involved**: Attacking mids, wingers, forwards, fullbacks
- **Passing pattern**: Direct, aggressive, chance-creating passes
- **Duration**: 2-4 passes typically
- **Key principle**: Create and finish (or repeat if chance isn't clear)

**Example sequence:**
```
AM → Winger (open space) → CB in box
→ Finish (shoot or cross)
```

### Principle 2: Support Angles and Positioning
The foundation of playing through lines is **constant support positioning** at each stage.

**What are support angles?**
A support angle is the position a teammate takes so the ball carrier can pass to them.

**Good support angles:**
- **Forward/Penetrating angle**: Position ahead of ball carrier (in direction of goal)
- **Sideways angle**: Position to the side (allows lateral pass to open new area)
- **Checking-back angle**: Position behind ball carrier (safety valve; allows retreat if blocked)

**Bad support angles:**
- **Parallel line**: Positioned at same depth as ball carrier (no progression, nowhere to go)
- **Too far**: More than 15-20 meters away (pass is risky; easily intercepted)
- **Too close**: Less than 3 meters away (defenders can cover both players)
- **Straight line**: In direct line between ball carrier and goal (congestion; no passing lane)

**Example of good support structure:**

```
        Striker (forward angle)
       /
Winger (penetrating) — AM (has ball) — FB (sideways)
       \
        DM (checking back - safety)
```

This gives the AM 4 passing options: forward, sideways, lateral deep, and backward.

**Positioning sequence:**
1. Ball carrier receives with support nearby
2. Support players position in good angles (forward, sideways, back)
3. Ball carrier passes to best option (usually forward or sideways)
4. New ball carrier immediately gets support in their area
5. Sequence continues

**Common positioning errors:**
- Everyone stands in line (parallel to goal); no space created
- Players bunch together (all within 5 meters); congestion
- No checking-back option (stuck if forward pass isn't available)
- Gaps in structure (player receives with no support nearby)

### Principle 3: Third-Man Runs (Breaking Lines Progressively)
A third-man run within the playing-through-lines sequence is a specific movement to break the next opposition line.

**The structure:**
- Player A has the ball
- Player B makes first pass (closer option)
- Player C makes a **timed run** into space behind an opposition defensive line
- Player B plays out to Player C (skipping an opposition line)
- Sequence continues with new ball carrier

**Why it works:**
- Opposition is focused on A and B
- C's run is into unmarked space
- B releases to C, breaking the next line
- Sequence has now broken TWO lines (A→B was first; B→C is second)

**Timing of the third-man run:**
- Third-man should run **as the second player is receiving** (or just before)
- Too early: Run arrives in space but second player isn't ready to play out
- Too late: Run happens after second player has to do something else with the ball
- Perfect timing: Third player arrives in space just as second player is looking to play out

**Example:**

```
Phase 1: CB → DM (first pass; not breaking a line yet)
Phase 2: DM receives, CB plays deep
         AM makes third-man RUN into space
         DM plays out to AM (breaking first midfield line)
Phase 3: AM receives in attacking space; continues sequence
```

### Principle 4: Progression vs. Possession
The decision at each phase: **Do we push forward or consolidate possession?**

**When to progress (keep moving forward):**
- Clear space/gap ahead (next line is broken or sparse)
- Numerical advantage in your area (you have more players than they can cover)
- Opposition is unorganized (pressing trap or poor shape)
- Time is right (early in match; time to score)

**When to consolidate (keep possession, sideways/lateral passes):**
- Opposition is well-organized (can't push forward safely)
- Space ahead is congested (risk of turnover if you push)
- You need to pull opposition out of shape first (tire them or drag defenders)
- Time is against you (need to control tempo)

**Balance:**
- Too much progression: Lose the ball; giveaway in advanced position
- Too much consolidation: Never create chances; game becomes static

**Elite teams alternate** between progression and consolidation based on opposition response.

## Position context (how it differs by role)

### Center-Back (Phase 1 - Build-Up Foundation)
**Role in playing through lines:**
- Start the sequence (first pass initiator)
- Often play the first 2-3 passes (short, safe)
- Support fullbacks during build-up
- Transition into supporting the DM when progressing

**Positioning approach:**
- Receive in deep area (comfortable)
- Play short to fullback or other CB
- Move to support fullback moving forward
- Ready to play back-pass if progression is blocked

**Example:**
CB has ball → plays to RB → moves to support RB → RB plays forward to DM

### Fullback (Phase 1-2 - Bridge Between Defense and Midfield)
**Role in playing through lines:**
- Support CB in build-up (receive and move forward)
- Break first opposition line by moving into midfield
- Support AM or DM in progression phase
- Create width and depth options

**Positioning approach:**
- Check back to receive from CB
- Move forward when receiving (not sideways)
- Position to support AM/DM
- Ready to cross or carry into attacking third

**Example:**
CB → FB (checks back) → FB moves forward → plays to AM (forward progress)

### Defensive Midfielder (Phase 2 - Midfield Progression)
**Role in playing through lines:**
- Receive from FB/CB
- Play first progressing pass (to AM or forward)
- Support runs for third-man breaks
- Serve as pivot (receiving multiple passes)

**Positioning approach:**
- Drop to receive from defense (checking back)
- Move forward when progressing
- Position between back line and attacking mids
- Always have 2-3 passing options

**Example:**
DM drops to receive → plays to FB (sideways) → receives again from FB
→ plays forward to AM (progression) → AM continues sequence

### Attacking Midfielder (Phase 2-3 - Breaking Into Attack)
**Role in playing through lines:**
- Receive from DM or FB
- Play penetrating pass (breaking lines forward)
- Make third-man runs to break opposition's last line
- Link defense to strikers

**Positioning approach:**
- Position in space between opposition midfield and defensive lines
- Move into space away from markers
- Make timed runs into attacking space
- Ready to play immediately after receiving

**Example:**
DM plays forward to AM → AM makes space → plays to winger or striker

### Fullback in Attack (Attacking Phase 3)
**Role in playing through lines:**
- Provide width at attacking third
- Support winger or make attacking run
- Create crossing opportunity
- Alternative passing option for AM

**Positioning approach:**
- Move forward as sequence progresses
- Position wider (away from congestion)
- Ready to cross or cut back
- Support striker's movement

### Striker / Forward (Phase 3 - Attack Finish)
**Role in playing through lines:**
- Final destination of sequence (not final recipient, but creates the danger)
- Make runs that allow through-ball finishes
- Support play in attacking area
- Finish or create the chance

**Positioning approach:**
- Move to create space (away from markers)
- Time runs to coincide with through-ball
- Position for shot or pass (create options for finishers)

## What GOOD looks like

**High-level execution (elite team):**
- Play 5-8 consecutive passes moving from defensive third to attacking third
- Maintain 90%+ pass completion while progressing
- Break opposition's first line with DM-to-AM pass (1-2 passes into progression phase)
- Break second line with third-man run (AM or FB to striker space)
- Create clear chance within 3-5 passes of entering attacking third
- Success rate 70%+ (sequences regularly lead to chances)
- Examples: Manchester City, Barcelona, elite possession-dominant teams

**Good execution (professional/high-level amateur):**
- Play 4-6 consecutive passes while progressing
- 75-80% pass completion during progression
- Sequences create space in midfield (if not clear attacking chance)
- Takes 6-8 passes to create clear chance (slower progression)
- Success rate 50-60%
- Examples: Championship teams, good academy teams

**Developing execution:**
- Struggle to string together more than 3-4 passes
- Pass completion drops under 70% (turnovers)
- Little progression (mostly sideways/lateral movement)
- Sequences often stall in midfield
- Success rate 30-40%
- Examples: U14-U16 academy players, developing semi-professional

## What POOR looks like

**Poor sequencing:**
- Passes don't flow (gaps between passes; dead stops)
- Poor support positioning (players aren't ready for passes)
- No clear progression (moves sideways/backward)
- Breaks down before reaching attacking third

**Poor execution:**
- Inaccurate passes (forced turnovers)
- Slow passes (opposition can reorganize)
- Poor first touch (loses momentum when receiving)
- Bad decision-making (shoots when should pass; passes when should shoot)

**Poor results:**
- Sequences lose the ball (turnover in midfield/attacking third)
- Sequences stall (stuck in midfield, no progression)
- Sequences create no chance (movement is predictable)
- Sequences are the same pattern repeatedly (opposition learns it)

## Key coaching points

### Structuring the Sequence
1. **Three phases**: Build-up (safety) → Progression (lines) → Attack (finish)
2. **Support angles are critical**: Forward, sideways, checking-back options at each pass
3. **Third-man runs break lines**: Timing of runs determines if sequence progresses
4. **Consolidate when needed**: Sideways passes are okay if forward progression is blocked

### Execution
5. **Firm, accurate passes**: Soft passes slow the sequence; inaccurate passes break it
6. **First touch into space**: Always move ball away from pressure
7. **Movement before the pass**: Support positions ready before ball is played
8. **Tempo/speed**: Sequence moves quickly (not frantically; controlled)

### Decision-Making
9. **Push or hold**: Read opposition; progression vs. consolidation
10. **When to finish**: Clear chance = finish; no clear chance = continue building
11. **Positioning for next action**: Always position for what comes next
12. **Escape if blocked**: If forward isn't available, sideways/back option is ready

## Teaching progression (unopposed → semi-opposed → conditioned game)

### Level 1: Unopposed (Sequencing and Positioning)
**Focus: Learning the passing patterns and support structure**

**Drill: "Build-Up Sequence" (10-15 minutes)**
- Setup: Defenders and DM practice short passing sequence from back
- Pattern: CB→CB→FB→CB (lateral) → DM (forward) → FB (support) = 6 passes
- Objective: Execute 10 sequences without loss of possession
- Progression:
  - Add fourth pass option
  - Increase distance (longer passes)
  - Add movement (players move after passing)

**Drill: "Progression Through Midfield" (10-15 minutes)**
- Setup: DM and AMs practice forward progression
- Pattern: DM receives → plays to AM → AM moves → DM supports → AM plays forward
- Objective: String together 4-5 passes progressing toward goal
- Progression:
  - Add width (fullbacks join)
  - Longer distances
  - More complex support positioning

**Drill: "Third-Man Run Timing" (10-15 minutes)**
- Setup: Three-player sequence practicing third-man timing
- Pattern: A passes to B, C makes timed run, B plays out to C
- Objective: Perfect the timing (C arrives in space as B is ready to play)
- Progression:
  - Speed increases
  - Run variations (different angles)
  - Add multiple third-man runs in sequence

**Drill: "Full Sequence: Back to Attack" (15-20 minutes)**
- Setup: Entire team practices full 8-pass sequence from back to goal
- Pattern: CB→FB→CB→DM→AM→Winger→Striker (8 passes)
- Objective: Execute with no loss of possession, clear progression
- Progression:
  - Speed increases
  - Multiple variations (different path patterns)
  - Add timing pressure (must complete in 20 seconds)

### Level 2: Semi-Opposed (Adding Light Pressure)
**Focus: Executing sequences under defensive pressure**

**Drill: "Build-Up Against Light Pressing" (10-15 minutes)**
- Setup: 1-2 light defenders press on the ball
- Defending team doesn't tackle; just provides token pressure
- Build-up team continues sequence
- Objective: Execute sequences with pressure present
- Progression:
  - Defenders become semi-active
  - Add more defenders
  - Increase pressing intensity

**Drill: "Progression Under Coordinated Press" (10-15 minutes)**
- Setup: 2-3 defenders organize pressing on progression phase
- Build-up team must find third-man runs to escape press
- Objective: Use third-man runs to break opposition lines while pressed
- Progression:
  - Pressing becomes more organized
  - More defenders added
  - Less time to execute (faster opposition)

**Drill: "Sequence Against Semi-Organized Defense" (15-20 minutes)**
- Setup: Defense is organized but doesn't press aggressively
- Build-up team plays full sequence against set defense
- Objective: String together 6-8 passes to create clear chance
- Progression:
  - Defense becomes more active
  - Add pressing triggers
  - Require finish after sequence

**Game 1: "Possession with Progression Requirement" (15-20 minutes)**
- Setup: 5v5 or 6v6 possession game
- Condition: Team must complete 5-pass sequence moving from one end to other
- Ball must progress through all three zones (back, middle, attack)
- Objective: Execute sequences while possession is contested
- Progression:
  - Increase defenders
  - Reduce touch limit
  - Add requirement for shots after progression

### Level 3: Conditioned Game / Semi-Opposed (Game-Realistic)
**Focus: Applying sequences in realistic game scenarios**

**Game 2: "Build-Up to Finish" (15-20 minutes)**
- Setup: 6v6 or 7v7 game with emphasis on build-up play
- Condition: Goals only count if they come from sequences that started from back third
- Objective: Execute full sequences (build-up + progression + attack) to score
- Progression:
  - Increase field size
  - Defending team becomes more organized
  - Add counter-press element (defense can transition)

**Game 3: "Full Field Playing Through Lines" (20-25 minutes)**
- Setup: 8v8 or 9v9 game, full field
- Coach awards bonus points for:
  - Completed 5-pass sequence (1 point)
  - Sequence resulting in shot (3 points)
  - Sequence resulting in goal (5 points)
- Objective: Apply sequences in full-match context with incentive
- Progression: Remove point system; play natural game

**Game 4: "Position-Specific Sequence Drill" (20 minutes)**
- Setup: Defenders and midfielders practice building and progressing sequences
- Scenario: Back four and mids build up, front three attack
- Build progression: Unopposed → 1 striker pressing → 2 strikers → small-sided game
- Objective: Develop sequences in position-specific context
- Progression: Increase pace; add decision elements; add counter-press

**Game 5: "Match Play with Build-Up Emphasis" (20-30 minutes)**
- Setup: Full 11v11 match with coach feedback on sequencing
- Coach pauses to highlight:
  - Good sequences
  - Where sequences broke down
  - What could have been different
- Objective: Apply in full-match context with learning feedback
- Progression: Play natural game without pauses
