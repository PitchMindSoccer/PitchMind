# Shooting & Finishing Fundamentals

## What it is
Shooting is the act of attempting to score by directing the ball toward goal. Finishing is the technical and tactical execution that converts chances into goals. A "shot" is defined by clear intent to score (not a pass or cross). Finishing encompasses: body shape at the moment of strike, shot placement (where the ball goes), shot power, timing of the shot, and composure under pressure. The underlying mechanics are UNIVERSAL across positions—what differs is the approach and frequency.

## Why it matters (player development angle)
Shooting is the final, highest-value action in soccer. A player who receives 5 chances per match but finishes 1 (20% conversion) vs. one who finishes 3 (60% conversion) creates 2 additional goals per match. Finishing separates:
- **Elite finishers**: Confident, technically sound, comfortable under pressure, high conversion rates
- **Good players**: Reliable finishers, some hesitation, context-dependent
- **Developing players**: Technical flaws (poor body shape, weak contact), poor decision-making (shooting when shouldn't), low confidence

For ALL positions, improving finishing is about:
1. **Body position** before the shot (balance, plant foot, hip position)
2. **Shot selection** (when to shoot vs. when to pass or move the ball)
3. **Placement vs. power** (accuracy vs. power trade-off)
4. **Composure** (executing under pressure, fatigue, crowded spaces)
5. **Movement timing** (arriving into scoring positions with momentum)

## Universal principles (applies to ALL positions)

### Principle 1: Shot Quality > Shot Quantity
Modern analytics use **Expected Goals (xG)** to measure shot quality. A shot from 8 meters centrally is ~0.25 xG (25% likelihood of goal). A shot from 20 meters wide is ~0.02 xG (2% likelihood). Elite finishers take HIGH-QUALITY shots, not just many shots.

**Universal rule**: A center-back or midfielder taking a shot from 25 meters has LOWER expected value than passing to a forward in the box. The shot is only appropriate if:
- No better passing option is available
- The opportunity is surprising/unexpected (goalkeeper out of position)
- A more central, closer shot isn't available in the next action

**Decision framework**:
- 0-6 meters, central: SHOOT (xG 0.40+)
- 6-12 meters, central: SHOOT or PASS (xG 0.15-0.40)
- 12-18 meters, central: Depends on goalkeeper position, defender pressure (xG 0.05-0.15)
- 18+ meters or wide: Only shoot if no better option (xG 0.01-0.05)

### Principle 2: Body Shape Determines Strike Quality
The strike begins with body positioning BEFORE the shot. Elite finishers position their body to maximize accuracy, power, and speed of execution.

**Body shape checklist**:
1. **Approach angle**: Approach the ball at 30-45° angle (not straight-on or too wide)
2. **Plant foot**: Place non-shooting foot 15-20cm from the ball, pointing toward target
3. **Hip/shoulder alignment**: Hips and shoulders face the general direction of shot, not backward-leaning
4. **Head down, eyes on ball**: Look at the spot on the ball where you want to strike
5. **Arms for balance**: Arms out to sides for balance, not tucked in
6. **Contact point**: Strike with inside of foot (control), laces (power), or outside foot (curve)

**Poor body shape examples**:
- Planting foot too close to ball → no power, poor direction
- Leaning backward → weak, inaccurate shot (ball flies high)
- Planting foot pointed away from target → the ball goes where your body points, not where you look
- Head up → likely to mis-strike, weak contact
- Rushed approach → no balance, wild shot

### Principle 3: Placement vs. Power Trade-Off
Elite finishers understand WHEN to prioritize accuracy vs. power.

**High-pressure scenarios** (goalkeeper rushing, defender closing):
- Prioritize placement (side netting is okay, keeper can't reach) over power
- Use inside foot for control, even if less powerful
- Aim for corners, not power shots that might be blocked or saved

**Open goal / slow goalkeeper** (goalkeeper out of position or slow):
- Power becomes more important (less time for recovery)
- Aim for the far corner with pace
- Even an off-target power shot is harder to stop

**Crowded penalty area**:
- Accuracy first (slot it past defenders, not blast it)
- Inside foot, side netting, low finish to avoid defenders' legs
- Speed of decision matters more than power of shot

### Principle 4: Composure Under Pressure
Composure is the ability to execute high-quality finishing when:
- Defender is closing you down
- Fatigue sets in (legs heavy, vision fuzzy)
- Match consequence is high (penalty areas, final minutes)
- Previous attempts haven't gone in (psychological pressure)

**Composure breakdown**:
- Rushing the shot (shooting before arriving into position)
- Over-thinking (hesitating, allowing defender to block)
- Panic (wild power shot instead of placed shot)
- Mechanical breakdown (poor body shape due to stress/fatigue)

**Building composure**: Repetition, practice under fatigue, practicing with pressure (coaches closing, time constraints), match experience, confidence from previous goals.

### Principle 5: Movement Before the Shot
Elite finishers score 2-3 passes BEFORE the final shot. The quality of the goal often depends on movement timing in the build-up.

**High-quality movements**:
- **Arrival with momentum**: Moving into space as the ball arrives, not static
- **Timing the run**: Arriving at exactly the right moment (not offside, not too early/late)
- **Creating space**: Quick body turn to create a yard of space before shooting
- **Layoff passes**: Receiving a pass and immediately playing a 1-touch layoff that creates a clearer shooting lane
- **Positioning before the chance**: Being in the right area of the pitch when teammates have the ball

**Poor movements**:
- Static finishing: Standing still and waiting for the ball (easy to defend)
- Late arrival: By the time you arrive, defender has blocked
- Crowded positioning: Finishing surrounded by defenders with no space
- Poor body position receiving: Receiving the ball on your back foot, unable to shoot quickly

## Position context (how it differs by role)

### Striker / Center-Forward
**Frequency**: Shoots 3-8 times per match  
**Position**: Central, between fullbacks, 18-yard box focus  
**Priority**: Positioning in box, making space for shot, high-quality finishes only

- Spends match focused on finishing areas
- Shoots from closer range (typical shot 12 meters)
- More pressure situations (marked by defenders in box)
- Needs: Strong off-the-ball movement to create space, ability to finish with first touch or one touch, composure in crowded box

### Winger / Wide Attacker
**Frequency**: Shoots 2-4 times per match  
**Position**: Wide, moving toward byline, 16-yard area focus  
**Priority**: Creating space with dribbling, cutting inside, finishing from medium range

- Often wide angles (less central positioning)
- Shoots from 16-20 meter range more often
- Flexibility: finish from wing position OR cut inside for central shot
- Needs: Ability to shoot on both feet, comfortable with off-balance finishing, quick decision-making (shoot vs. pass to striker)

### Attacking Midfielder / Number 10
**Frequency**: Shoots 2-4 times per match  
**Position**: Central, between lines, 20-yard focus  
**Priority**: High xG opportunities from playmaking position, shooting or passing decision

- Creates shooting opportunities for others
- Shoots from medium range (18-25 meters)
- Often has time to shoot (less crowded than box)
- Needs: Long-range finishing accuracy, ability to place shot from distance, shooting timing (knowing when to shoot vs. release forward pass)

### Box-to-Box Midfielder
**Frequency**: Shoots 1-3 times per match  
**Position**: Central, deep to attacking, variable range  
**Priority**: Arriving late into box, power finishes, set pieces

- Scores from late runs into box
- Shoots from 18-25 meters (less central finishing)
- Often has less time/space than strikers (arriving quickly)
- Needs: Power finishing, timing of late runs, ability to finish off-balance

### Fullback
**Frequency**: Shoots 0.5-1.5 times per match  
**Position**: Wide, overlapping, 20+ meter range  
**Priority**: Set pieces, overlapping runs into wide box

- Rarely shoots in open play (priority is defensive/possession)
- Shots usually from wide angles (low xG) or set pieces
- When shooting in open play, often from distance (overlapping position)
- Needs: Composure when shooting (infrequent opportunity), accuracy from distance, ability to cross instead of shoot

### Center-Back / Defender
**Frequency**: Shoots 0.2-0.5 times per match  
**Position**: Deep, set pieces, rare build-up opportunities  
**Priority**: Set pieces (corners), rare long-range attempts

- Almost never shoots in open play
- Set piece finishing (headers, rare free kicks)
- When attempting shot, usually long distance (low xG)
- Needs: Set piece finishing (heading), comfort taking shot when opportunity presents, not overthinking rare chances

## What GOOD looks like

**Excellent striker finishing**:
- Takes 5 shots per match, 3-4 are high xG (>0.15), converts 50%+ of high-xG chances
- Body shape is precise every time (perfect plant foot, hips aligned)
- Mix of placement and power (knows which approach for each situation)
- Finishes under pressure without panic (defender closing, crowd noise, prior misses)
- Movement into shooting position is timed perfectly (arriving with momentum)
- First-touch finishing (receives and shoots in one motion) is reliable
- Comfortable finishing off both feet
- Confidence visible (takes shots decisively, doesn't hesitate or overly relax)

**Excellent midfielder finishing**:
- Takes 2-3 shots per match, 1-2 are high xG
- Converts 30-40% of realistic chances (lower conversion than strikers is normal)
- Knows shot placement (side netting, near post) to avoid defenders
- Mix of long-range and penalty-area finishing
- Quick decision-making (shoot vs. pass happening within 0.5 seconds)
- Power finishing on late runs is reliable
- Composure on rare chances (doesn't panic or overthink)

**Excellent winger finishing**:
- Takes 2-3 shots per match, mixes angles (wide and cut-inside)
- Comfortable shooting off both feet
- Cut-inside finishes are accurate and placed
- Wide-angle finishing (when crossing isn't available) is composed
- Quick shooting decision (takes shot before defender sets)
- Conversion rate 25-35% (good for position, considering wide angles)

**Excellent fullback finishing**:
- Overlapping runs result in quality chances (not wild attempts)
- Set piece finishing (if headers) is reliable
- When shooting in open play, it's high-quality (not desperation)
- Doesn't over-shoot (knows crossing is higher xG than long shots)
- Rare finishing attempts are composed, not panicked

## What POOR looks like

**Poor striker finishing**:
- Takes 5 shots per match but mostly low xG (<0.10) or creates shots from poor positions
- Body shape inconsistent (rushing shots, poor plant foot, leaning back)
- Wild power shots instead of placed finishes
- Hesitates or overthinks (takes too long before shooting, allowing defenders to block)
- Misses open goals (panic, over-power, mechanical breakdown)
- Finishing deteriorates under pressure (late match, defender closing, previous misses)
- Off-balance finishing is unreliable
- Confidence fragile (after miss, stops shooting or becomes overly cautious)

**Poor midfielder finishing**:
- Shoots from poor positions (25+ meters, wide, low xG) instead of moving ball closer
- Shot selection poor (shoots when passing to striker would be better)
- Power over placement (blasts when accuracy needed)
- Hesitates on chances (allows defender to block)
- Conversion rate <10% (poor even for position)
- No set piece threat (free kicks, corners result in poor finishing)

**Poor winger finishing**:
- Always shoots with same foot (can't finish off weaker foot)
- Wide-angle shots are inaccurate (ball flies over/wide)
- Cut-inside finishing is off-balance or rushed
- Doesn't understand placement (always power shooting)
- Over-shooting decision (shoots from poor position when pass to striker available)
- Confidence issues (after miss, avoids shooting)

**Poor fullback finishing**:
- When shooting in open play, it's desperation/panic
- Over-committing to shooting instead of crossing
- Set piece finishing is unreliable
- Wild attempts that have no chance
- Doesn't calculate shooting decision properly (low-xG attempts)

## Key coaching points

### Technical Coaching
1. **Plant foot placement**: Non-shooting foot 15-20cm from ball, pointing toward target. This determines shot direction more than anything else.
2. **Contact point**: Inside foot (control/accuracy), laces (power), outside foot (curve). Young players should master inside foot first.
3. **Follow-through**: Shooting leg continues toward target after contact (not stopping at impact). This drives the ball forward with power.
4. **Head position**: Eyes on ball at moment of strike, not looking at goalkeeper or target. Lift head AFTER contact.
5. **Approach angle**: 30-45° angle, not straight-on or too wide. Test this with cones—approach from different angles and see which produces best contact.

### Tactical Coaching
1. **Shot selection decision tree**: Before shooting, ask: "Is this shot high xG (>0.15)? If no, is there a better option (pass, dribble, move ball)? If no better option, is goalkeeper vulnerable?" This trains decision-making.
2. **Placement vs. power framework**: Crowded box = place it. Open goal = power shot. Goalkeeper rushing = low shot to side netting.
3. **Composure under pressure**: Simulate pressure in training. Coaches closing, limited time, match-consequence scenarios. Repetition builds composure.
4. **Movement into shooting position**: Track movement patterns 2-3 passes before the shot. Are players arriving with momentum? Static? Late?
5. **Set piece finishing**: Organize shooting practice after specific set pieces (corners, free kicks, throw-ins). These are high-xG opportunities.

### Development Stages
**U10-U12**: 
- Introduce plant foot, follow-through, basic body shape
- Comfort shooting from both feet (no preference)
- Simple decision: if 1v1 with goalkeeper, take shot
- High frequency practice (every session has shooting)

**U12-U14**:
- Refine body shape (consistency, balance)
- Introduce shot placement (side netting, accuracy over power)
- Decision-making: when to shoot vs. when to pass/move ball
- Scenario practice: crowded box, defender closing, goalkeeper rushing

**U14-U16**:
- Advanced finishing (off-balance, first touch, under pressure)
- Placement vs. power decision-making becomes automatic
- Set piece finishing specialization
- High-pressure simulation (match context, fatigue)

**U16+**:
- Elite body shape precision (every shot technically sound)
- Composure in match situations (fatigue, pressure, consequence)
- Position-specific finishing (winger vs. midfielder vs. striker)
- Conversion rate tracking and improvement targets

### Practice Structures
1. **Repetition finishing**: Shots with no pressure, focus on technique. 20-30 shots per session to build muscle memory.
2. **Pressure finishing**: Coach/teammate closing, limited time, match-realistic scenarios. Builds composure.
3. **Scenario finishing**: After specific movements (overlapping runs, cutbacks, through balls). Teaches timing and approach angles.
4. **Fatigue finishing**: Finishing circuits with running component. Tests composure when legs are heavy.
5. **Set piece finishing**: Practicing after corners, free kicks, throw-ins. High-xG situations deserve focused work.

### Data Metrics (if available)
- **Shots per 90**: How often player attempts shots
- **xG per 90**: Quality of shots (high xG = good positioning and selection)
- **Conversion rate**: Goals ÷ shots (position-dependent: strikers 15-30%, midfielders 5-15%, defenders <5%)
- **xG over/underperformance**: Actual goals vs. expected goals (positive = finishing above average, negative = finishing below average)
- **Shot placement**: Percentage of shots on target, to corners, to sides (high percentage = good placement)
