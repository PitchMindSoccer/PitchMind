# Pressing Theory

## What it is
Pressing is the collective defensive act of applying pressure to the opposition when they have possession. It's not random aggression—it's a structured, decision-driven system where players close down space and time available to the ball carrier based on specific triggers and team positioning rules. Modern pressing includes both immediate pressure (man-oriented) and positional pressure (space-oriented), depending on the defensive model.

## Why it matters (player development angle)
Young players need to understand WHEN to press, not just HOW to press. A player who presses without understanding the trigger or collective structure creates defensive chaos. Elite pressing requires:
- **Press resistance**: knowing your defensive line won't be exposed if the ball gets past you
- **Trigger recognition**: understanding what activates pressure (ball location, player receiving, formation shape)
- **Collective timing**: pressing only works if teammates know you're coming and cover the space you leave
- **Recovery pace**: being able to shift quickly from pressing to covering when the play shifts direction

## What GOOD looks like
**Systematic pressing:**
- Defenders step up together, not individually
- Press is triggered by clear signals (ball in certain zone, sideways pass, specific receiver)
- If first press is beaten, the next line of defense is already set and compact
- Players maintain defensive shape while applying pressure—they don't chase the ball in straight lines
- After pressure is applied, if the ball escapes, defenders quickly reset to a mid-block or drop back as a unit
- Pressing intensity matches the match situation (higher in final third, less risky in own half)

**Example: Bologna's systematic approach (Serie A 2025/26)**
- Reduce space at every point of reception across the pitch
- No "waiting phases"—every opposition reception is attacked
- High collective coherence compensates for the risk of individual duels

## What POOR looks like
- **Uncoordinated pressing**: One player rushes forward while others stay deep, creating gaps
- **Reactive pressing**: Chasing the ball after it's already been released, rather than cutting off options proactively
- **No trigger**: Players press randomly, leaving the defense exposed
- **Lack of compactness**: Creating large gaps between defensive units that opponents exploit
- **Over-committing**: Pressing too high without cover, allowing dangerous counter-attacks
- **Static recovery**: After a press is beaten, players slowly walk back instead of immediately resetting shape

## Key coaching points
- **Establish clear triggers**: When/where the team presses (e.g., "press in attacking third," "press if ball goes sideways to fullback")
- **Teach press resistance**: Show defenders that if they press, teammates are covering the space they leave
- **Practice collective timing**: Pressing only works if 2-3 players move together, not one in isolation
- **Pressure data metrics**: Use StatsBomb pressure data to assess:
  - How often opposition actions occur under pressure in each zone
  - Whether pressured actions lead to possession loss
  - Which opponents struggle under pressure (and which thrive)
- **Positional pressing vs. man-marking**: Some teams (Como, Roma) use zonal pressing to control passing lanes; others (Bologna) are more man-oriented
- **Recovery mechanics**: When the ball escapes the initial press, immediately shift to a mid-block rather than scattered defending
- **Match context**: Adjust pressing intensity based on score, opponent quality, and fatigue
- **Individual press resistance**: Players must be comfortable receiving under pressure and executing quick passes or carries to escape pressure
