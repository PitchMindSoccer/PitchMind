# Overlapping and Underlapping Runs: Fullback Attacking Movement

## What it is
Overlapping and underlapping runs are **specific types of off-ball movement by fullbacks** (and sometimes midfielders) to create attacking advantage on the flank.

**Overlapping run**: A fullback (or defender) runs past a winger/player on the flank to receive the ball further forward, effectively "overlapping" their position.
- Creates a 2v1 situation on the flank (2 attackers vs. 1 defender)
- Fullback arrives in space to cross or shoot
- Winger can play without being tightly marked

**Underlapping run**: A fullback runs **inside** (toward the center) rather than down the line, appearing at an angle to receive the ball. 
- Creates numerical advantage in central/inside area
- Used when the winger is already occupied or when central space is more valuable
- Often involves a back-pass or square pass to the fullback

Key difference: **Overlapping is about beating the fullback down the line; underlapping is about creating central play.**

## Why it matters (player development)
Modern soccer uses fullbacks as primary attacking assets. Teams that execute overlapping/underlapping effectively:
- Create 2v1 overloads on the flank (especially dangerous)
- Use fullbacks in shooting/crossing positions
- Draw opposition defenders out of position (creates space elsewhere)
- Score from wider areas (overlaps create crossing opportunities)

Young players who master these runs:
- **Elite fullbacks**: Execute overlaps/underlaps with timing and decision-making; 75%+ success rate
- **Good players**: Execute overlaps when coached; reasonable timing; 60% success
- **Developing players**: Run timing is poor; often blocked or not used effectively; 40% success

This separates fullbacks who are only defensive players from fullbacks who are **attacking threats**.

## Universal principles (applies to ALL positions)

### Principle 1: When to Overlap vs. Underlap
The choice between overlapping and underlapping depends on **game situation**.

**Use OVERLAP when:**

**Situation 1: Winger is isolated and tightly marked**
- Opposition fullback is pressing the winger tightly
- Winger needs support to escape pressure
- Overlap creates a 2v1 situation
- Fullback can receive in space to cross or shoot

**Situation 2: Space down the line is more valuable than central space**
- Opposition is defending centrally (compact)
- Touchline is relatively open
- Overlap allows fullback to get to the goal line (better crossing position)

**Situation 3: You want the winger to stay wide and drift inward**
- Overlapping fullback allows winger to play more centrally/inward
- Creates space for winger to be a playmaker (not just crosser)
- Fullback provides width; winger provides creativity

**Situation 4: Defensive setup is deep (low block)**
- Opposition is sitting deep
- Less central congestion means overlapping down the line is easier
- Overlap creates wide crossing opportunity from good position

**Example match situation:**
Right winger is isolated and tightly marked by opposition fullback. Right fullback overlaps, creating 2v1 (winger + fullback vs. fullback). Winger plays to fullback who crosses from the goal line.

**Use UNDERLAP when:**

**Situation 1: Central play is more dangerous than wide play**
- Opposition is spread wide (open central area)
- Underlapping fullback can receive and play centrally
- Creates inside-to-outside passing lane

**Situation 2: Midfield needs support (congestion in central area)**
- Defending opposition are pressing the winger
- Central midfield has space
- Underlapping fullback creates an extra midfielder option

**Situation 3: Winger is in a good position and needs less support**
- Winger has space and isn't marked tightly
- Underlap is unnecessary; central support is better
- Fullback drops back to support midfield or create central link

**Situation 4: Transition moment (switching play)**
- Ball is transitioning from one side to the other
- Underlapping fullback can create link in central area
- Useful when switching play from right to left (and vice versa)

**Example match situation:**
Left winger has decent space but isn't in immediate crossing position. Left fullback underlaps, appearing in central area. This creates an inside pass option for the winger or a central link to midfielders.

### Principle 2: Timing of the Overlap/Underlap Run
**Timing is the most critical element** of overlap/underlap success. A poorly-timed run is useless.

**Perfect timing:**
- Fullback's run arrives at receiving position **just as the winger is releasing the ball**
- Ball and fullback arrive at the same time (fullback receives in stride)
- Fullback is already moving when receiving (momentum into next action)
- Opportunity is fresh (opponent hasn't repositioned)

**Too early:**
- Fullback arrives at receiving position before winger is ready to play
- Winger has to wait/hold the ball
- Opponent fullback sees the overlap and adjusts (marking the fullback instead)
- Running space is lost

**Too late:**
- Fullback arrives after winger has already played the ball elsewhere
- Overlap opportunity is wasted
- Fullback is jogging into empty space

**Timing cues:**
- Fullback should **start running as the winger is receiving the ball** (or just before)
- Run should take 1-1.5 seconds to reach receiving position (typical 15-20 meters)
- Winger should look to play the fullback within 0.5-1 second of receiving

**Common timing errors:**
- Fullback runs too early (arrives, waits, opportunity is lost)
- Fullback runs too late (ball is already played elsewhere)
- Fullback doesn't commit to the run (tentative movement; easy to defend)
- Winger doesn't recognize the overlap (doesn't look for the fullback's run)

### Principle 3: How the Ball Carrier Sets Up the Run
The **winger's role** in setting up the overlap/underlap is critical. Without proper setup, the run is useless.

**Setting up an overlap (winger's responsibility):**

**Step 1: Receive with awareness**
- Winger should know the fullback is about to run
- Look for the fullback's run developing
- Know how much space is available for the fullback

**Step 2: Shield/Control**
- Winger receives and immediately shields the ball (body between ball and defender)
- This creates time for the fullback's run to develop
- Prevents the defender from immediately pressing the winger

**Step 3: Play the ball when fullback is arriving**
- Time the pass to the fullback's arrival (not early, not late)
- Pass should be firm (not soft; allows fullback to continue in stride)
- Pass into space ahead of the fullback (for momentum)

**Step 4: Support the fullback's next action**
- After playing the fullback, winger should move to support
- If fullback crosses, winger should be in position to finish
- If fullback carries, winger should be an option for a return pass

**Setting up an underlap (winger's responsibility):**

**Step 1: Receive and assess space**
- Winger should recognize when central space is valuable
- Know that fullback is underlapping (coming inside)
- Understand that underlap is an alternative to overlap

**Step 2: Play a back-pass or square pass**
- Underlap usually involves a back-pass or sideways pass (not forward)
- Pass allows fullback to receive with space to play forward
- Creates a reset opportunity (fullback can progress from central area)

**Step 3: Reposition after pass**
- After passing to underlapping fullback, winger should move
- Can check back to support or spread width (create space for fullback)
- Creates passing options for fullback

**Winger errors that kill overlaps/underlaps:**
- Not playing the ball to the fullback (pretending the fullback doesn't exist)
- Playing too early (fullback isn't ready yet)
- Playing too late (fullback run has already ended)
- Playing with wrong weight (too soft, fullback can't control; too hard, runs past fullback)
- Not moving to support the fullback after the pass

### Principle 4: Decision at the End of the Run
What does the fullback do when they receive from an overlap/underlap?

**Receiving from an overlap (fullback down the line):**

**Option 1: Cross (Most Common)**
- Fullback receives near the goal line
- Immediately crosses (low cross, cutback, or high cross)
- Teammates should be in position to finish
- Objective: Create immediate chance

**Option 2: Cut Inside (If Space Available)**
- Fullback receives with space to cut inside
- Dribbles away from touchline; creates shooting opportunity
- Useful if goal line is congested (can't cross effectively)

**Option 3: One-Touch Finish (If Close Enough)**
- Fullback receives very close to goal
- One-touch shot if angle is good
- Rare but devastating if executed

**Option 4: Pass Back to Winger (If Marked)**
- If fullback receives but is immediately marked
- Play back to winger who is now in space
- Creates a second opportunity

**Receiving from an underlap (fullback inside):**

**Option 1: Play Forward (To Striker or Attacking Midfielder)**
- Underlapping fullback receives in central area
- Immediately plays forward pass
- Creates penetrating option

**Option 2: Play Sideways (Spread the Field)**
- Underlapping fullback can spread the field with lateral pass
- Creates space for wide players
- Used to reset if central play is blocked

**Option 3: Carry Forward (If Space Available)**
- Fullback receives in central area with space to dribble
- Carries the ball forward to create shooting position
- Useful if opposition is unorganized

**Option 4: Play Back (If Pressed)**
- If fullback is immediately marked upon receiving
- Play back to reset possession
- Try another approach

**Common errors after receiving:**
- Too many touches (loses momentum; defenders catch up)
- Wrong decision (crosses when should play forward; holds when should shoot)
- Poor execution (inaccurate cross, weak pass, or poor shot)
- Doesn't finish (creates opportunity but doesn't maximize it)

## Position context (how it differs by role)

### Fullback (Primary Player)
**This is their core responsibility.** Overlapping and underlapping are how fullbacks create attacking play.

**Overlap approach:**
- Timing run to winger's reception
- Sprint down the line in open space
- Receive near goal line
- Cross or shoot

**Underlap approach:**
- Timing run inside (toward center)
- Receive in space between opposition lines
- Play forward or sideways

**Position-specific considerations:**
- Right fullback overlaps: Down right line toward opponent's goal
- Left fullback overlaps: Down left line toward opponent's goal
- Either fullback can underlap (more central role)

### Winger (Supporting Player)
**Wingers facilitate overlaps/underlaps through intelligent play.**

**Role in overlap:**
- Play the ball to the overlapping fullback
- Move to support the fullback's next action
- Be ready for a return pass

**Role in underlap:**
- Recognize when underlap is better than overlap
- Play back-pass to underlapping fullback
- Reposition to support

**Winger errors:**
- Don't recognize the run (look down; miss the fullback)
- Play to the wrong option (ignore the fullback)
- Poor timing of the pass

### Central Midfielder (Secondary Support)
**Midfielders can support overlaps/underlaps** by creating space or providing additional options.

**Role in overlap:**
- Create space on the flank for fullback to receive
- Be available for a return pass if fullback is blocked
- Support the fullback's next action

**Role in underlap:**
- Recognize when fullback is underlapping
- Provide support option in central area
- Be available for a pass from underlapping fullback

### Striker / Attacking Midfielder (Target Player)
**Forwards are targets for overlapping fullbacks.**

**Role in overlap:**
- Be in position to receive a cross
- Be ready to finish
- Create space for the fullback to cross

## What GOOD looks like

**High-level execution (elite fullback):**
- Recognizes overlap/underlap opportunity immediately
- Times run perfectly (arrives as ball is released)
- Receives in space and executes next action (cross, shoot, play forward) within 1 touch
- Execution success rate 80%+ (overlaps/underlaps work most of the time)
- Creates clear crossing or shooting opportunities
- Examples: Trent Alexander-Arnold, Andy Robertson (Liverpool), elite attacking fullbacks

**Good execution (professional/high-level amateur):**
- Recognizes overlap/underlap opportunity (sometimes coach-prompted)
- Times run reasonably well (mostly synchronized with pass)
- Executes next action with 1-2 touches
- Success rate 60-70%
- Creates reasonable crossing opportunities
- Examples: Championship fullbacks, good academy fullbacks

**Developing execution:**
- Recognizes overlap/underlap inconsistently
- Timing is poor (often too early or too late)
- Executes next action with 2+ touches (slower than optimal)
- Success rate 40-50%
- Often loses opportunity or is blocked
- Examples: U14-U16 academy players, developing fullbacks

## What POOR looks like

**Poor recognition:**
- Fullback doesn't make overlap/underlap run (stays defensive)
- Winger doesn't recognize the run (doesn't play the fullback)
- Run is made at wrong time (during wrong phase of play)

**Poor timing:**
- Fullback arrives too early (has to wait; opportunity disappears)
- Fullback arrives too late (ball is already played elsewhere)
- Run is half-committed (tentative; easy for defender to read)

**Poor execution:**
- Winger doesn't play the fullback (looks down; misses the run)
- Pass is inaccurate (fullback can't control)
- Pass is wrong weight (too soft or too hard)
- Fullback can't execute next action (receives under immediate pressure)

**Poor results:**
- Overlap/underlap is defended (opposition fullback marks the run)
- Opportunity is wasted (fullback receives but can't do anything with it)
- Turnover occurs (poor pass or poor first touch)
- No advantage created (overlap doesn't lead to chance)

## Key coaching points

### Recognition & Decision
1. **Overlap = down the line attacking** (winger can play inside)
2. **Underlap = central support** (winger can stay wide)
3. **Timing is everything** (fullback arrives as ball is released)
4. **Setup is critical** (winger must shield and play at right moment)

### Execution
5. **Firm, not soft** (fullback needs control when receiving)
6. **Fullback commits to the run** (no tentative movements)
7. **Winger looks for the fullback** (awareness before receiving)
8. **Next action is immediate** (cross, shoot, or play forward)

### Common Errors
9. **Too early = waiting** (opportunity lost while fullback waits)
10. **Too late = wasted run** (fullback runs into empty space)
11. **Winger ignores the run** (doesn't look for or play fullback)
12. **Fullback makes a poor decision** (shoots when should cross, holds when should play)

## Teaching progression (unopposed → semi-opposed → conditioned game)

### Level 1: Unopposed (Timing and Movement)
**Focus: Learning the run and timing**

**Drill: "Overlap Run Timing" (10-15 minutes)**
- Setup: Fullback and winger practice overlap without defenders
- Winger receives pass from coach; fullback times run
- Winger plays to fullback; fullback completes action (cross/shoot)
- Objective: Perfect the timing (fullback arrives as ball is released)
- Progression:
  - Increase pace
  - Winger has to control first before playing fullback
  - Vary receiving position

**Drill: "Underlap Run Timing" (10-15 minutes)**
- Setup: Fullback and winger practice underlap without defenders
- Winger receives; fullback times inside run
- Winger plays back-pass to fullback; fullback completes action
- Objective: Timing of underlap run
- Progression:
  - Increase pace
  - Fullback must play forward (not sideways)
  - Multiple passing options for fullback

**Drill: "Run Commitment" (5-10 minutes)**
- Setup: Fullback practices running at pace
- Objective: Full-speed, committed runs (not tentative)
- Sprint to line; sprint to central area
- Progression:
  - Combine run with receiving and first touch
  - Increase run length

### Level 2: Semi-Opposed (Adding Light Pressure)
**Focus: Executing under defensive pressure**

**Drill: "Overlap with Passive Defense" (10-15 minutes)**
- Setup: Opposition fullback provides light pressure
- Opposition fullback doesn't tackle but tries to mark the run
- Fullback must time overlap to beat the press
- Objective: Execute overlap despite defensive pressure
- Progression:
  - Defender becomes more active
  - Add second defender (midfielder)
  - Increase pressure intensity

**Drill: "Underlap Against Organized Defense" (10-15 minutes)**
- Setup: Opposition midfielders organize; fullback underlaps
- Opposition defends central area
- Fullback must find space to receive and play
- Objective: Execute underlap despite defending
- Progression:
  - Defenders organize more tightly
  - Add pressing triggers
  - Limit space further

**Drill: "1v1 Fullback Defending Overlap" (10-15 minutes)**
- Setup: Opposition fullback defends against the overlap
- Attacking fullback must beat opposition fullback with timing/technique
- Winger and fullback execute overlap under 1v1 pressure
- Objective: Overlap against realistic 1v1 fullback defending
- Progression:
  - Opposition becomes more organized (2v1 or team defending)
  - Add counter-attack element

### Level 3: Conditioned Game / Semi-Opposed (Game-Realistic)
**Focus: Applying overlaps/underlaps in realistic game scenarios**

**Game 1: "Flank Attack with Overlaps/Underlaps" (15-20 minutes)**
- Setup: 4v4 or 5v5 with emphasis on flank play
- Condition: Goals only count if they're scored from an overlap or underlap
- Objective: Recognize and execute overlaps/underlaps; create chances from them
- Progression:
  - Increase field size
  - Add more players
  - Defending team becomes more organized

**Game 2: "Position-Specific Flank Drill" (15-20 minutes)**
- Setup: Fullbacks and wingers practice overlaps/underlaps
- Scenario: Flank play only (no central play); focus on combinations
- Build: Unopposed → 1 defender → 2 defenders → small-sided game
- Objective: Develop overlaps/underlaps in position-specific context
- Progression: Increase pace; add more defenders

**Game 3: "Full Match with Overlap/Underlap Emphasis" (20-25 minutes)**
- Setup: 8v8 or 11v11 game
- Coach awards bonus points for:
  - Successful overlap (1 point)
  - Successful underlap (1 point)
  - Overlap/underlap leading to shot (3 points)
  - Overlap/underlap leading to goal (5 points)
- Objective: Apply overlaps/underlaps in full-match context
- Progression: Remove point system; play natural game

**Game 4: "Real Match with Flank Focus" (20-30 minutes)**
- Setup: 11v11 match with coaching emphasis on flank play
- Coach provides feedback on:
  - Overlap/underlap timing
  - Winger's role in setting up run
  - Fullback's decision after receiving
- Objective: Apply in realistic match with learning feedback
- Progression: Play without coach interruption
