# Ball Progression: Passing vs. Carrying

## What it is
Ball progression is the act of moving the ball closer to the opponent's goal. Teams have two primary methods: **passing** (moving the ball from one player to another) and **carrying** (dribbling with the ball at your feet). The ratio and selection of these methods is not random—it's a strategic choice based on opposition pressure, available space, player quality, and the team's playing model. Some leagues (Bundesliga) favor passing; others (Ligue 1) favor carrying.

## Why it matters (player development angle)
Young players often have a bias toward one method or the other. Understanding WHEN to pass and WHEN to carry is critical for decision-making. This includes:
- **Reading space**: Is there space to carry? Or should I move the ball quickly via pass?
- **Reading pressure**: Am I under immediate pressure? (Carry to escape) Or do I have time? (Look for the progressive pass)
- **Knowing team model**: Does my team want me to progress via pass or carry in this zone?
- **Risk assessment**: A progressive carry in tight space is higher risk but can break defensive lines; a progressive pass is lower risk but slower
- **Technical execution**: Some players are elite passers, others elite carriers—know your strengths and use them

## What GOOD looks like
**Elite passing-oriented teams** (Arsenal, Bundesliga teams, Liverpool):
- Use passes to progress in buildup phase
- Carry more selectively in final third
- Pass accuracy is high even under pressure
- Line-breaking passes available frequently

**Elite carrying-oriented teams** (Ligue 1, PSG, Manchester City, Barcelona):
- Use carries to get up the pitch quickly, especially in transition
- Press resistance strong—not losing ball despite being in tight space
- Carries often precede shots or dangerous final-third actions
- Efficient—fewer touches to progress

**Context-dependent progression** (Brentford, Fulham):
- Pass-heavy team structure with specific carry players (e.g., Fulham uses Willian to carry)
- Opponents can't just mark one player—have to account for multiple progressors
- Flexibility in how progression happens

## What POOR looks like
- **Forced carries**: Trying to dribble through congestion when a pass would be faster
- **Backward progression**: Passing/carrying away from goal to avoid pressure instead of finding forward options
- **Predictable pattern**: Always pass or always carry—opposition reads and cuts off the obvious next action
- **Isolation**: Progressing with ball but no teammates ahead ready to receive
- **Loss of possession**: Attempting progression in tight space and losing the ball in dangerous area
- **Slow progression**: Taking too many touches to move the ball 25 yards forward (inefficient)

## Key coaching points
- **Develop both skills**: Young players need competence in both passing and carrying; don't specialize too early
- **Read pressure**: Under pressure, carrying (if press-resistant) escapes more quickly than looking for a pass; with space, a pass is often faster
- **Understand team model**: Does your manager want progression via pass or carry in each zone? This should be clear
- **Know your progressors**: In each position, who are your primary pass progressors and carry progressors?
- **Press resistance**: The ability to complete passes or carries under pressure is a critical skill for both methods
- **Line-breaking vs. safety**: Teach the risk/reward of a forward pass into traffic vs. a safe sideways pass
- **Efficiency metric**: It's not just about getting the ball forward; it's about doing it in the fewest touches/passes possible
- **Positioning for progression**: 
  - For passing: Have receiving options ahead and wide
  - For carrying: Have space to move into and teammates ready to receive
- **Data analysis**: Look at progressive pass/carry ratios in each zone and against each opponent type

## Statistical context
- Ratio of progressive passes to carries ranges from ~55% to ~75% across elite European teams
- Bundesliga: ~75% passes, ~25% carries (pass-heavy)
- Ligue 1: ~45% passes, ~55% carries (carry-heavy)
- La Liga: Mixed—carries to progress up pitch, passes in final third
- Premier League: Mixed—carries in penalty box, passes elsewhere
- Scottish Premiership: More varied than stereotype suggests
