### Concentration/Focus Measurement Framework

**Observable markers codified:**
- Pre-action scanning frequency (time before receiving)
- Decision-making speed (lag between seeing problem and solving it)
- Execution consistency (degradation under pressure)
- Mistake recovery time (benchmark: elite <20 sec, average 2-3 min, poor 5+ min)
- Fatigue effect timing (concentration lapses increase minute 70-90)
- Age-appropriate expectations (U10: 1-2 lapses per 10 min normal; U18+: <1 per 30 min expected)

**Scoring integration:** Concentration acts as a **multiplier on technical ability**. A 75-technique player with 90-concentration may score 80-85 overall; same player with 55-concentration may score 60-65 overall.

## Lessons Learned (Leo's Meta-Reflection)

1. **Observable markers are critical:** For concentration and defensive organization, defining exactly "what it looks like on video" was more important than just explaining the concept.

2. **Decision frameworks > rules:** Rather than saying "always overlap" or "never press," decision matrices (when to overlap, when to underlap, when to stay) are more useful for coaches and scoring.

3. **Age appropriateness matters:** Same action (focus lapse, jockeying error) has different meaning at U10 vs. U18. Scoring must be tier-aware from the start.

4. **Synthesis > sources:** Modern soccer knowledge isn't in PDFs anymore; it's in elite teams' patterns. Synthesizing from modern pressing systems (Rangnick, Pep, Tuchel) was more valuable than trying to find traditional coaching books.

5. **Concentration as multiplier:** Recognizing that focus/consistency acts as a multiplier on technical ability was a breakthrough. A technically gifted but inconsistent player is essentially "lower tier" than a solid player with elite consistency.
