## 1. POSITION OVERVIEW AND ROLE IN MODERN FOOTBALL

The Striker (ST) — also referred to as the Centre Forward (CF) or Number 9 — is the primary attacking reference point of any team. In the modern game, the striker is one of the most tactically varied positions on the pitch. No longer simply a box presence waiting for crosses, the contemporary striker must function as a link-up player, a pressing initiator, a channel runner, a hold-up target, and a finisher — often within the same possession sequence.

The role has evolved across three dominant archetypes in modern football:

**The Target Striker (Traditional 9):** Physically imposing, dominant in aerial duels, capable of holding the ball with back to goal, creating second-ball opportunities for midfielders arriving late. This type sets the table for others as much as they finish themselves.

**The Movement Striker (False or Fluid 9):** Drops into pockets between the lines, pulling center-backs out of position and creating space in behind for runners. Often interchanges with attacking midfielders or wide forwards. Their intelligence — not size — defines them.

**The In-Behind Striker (Box-to-Box Runner):** Exploits defensive lines with diagonal and vertical runs, demanding the ball in space rather than to feet. Lives off the timing of through-balls, high defensive lines, and transition moments.

Most elite youth and college strikers blend elements of all three, and PitchMind evaluates decisions against these hybrid demands. The system must recognize which archetype a player is executing in a given moment and whether their decisions are optimal for that role in that context.

### Why the ST Position Is Central to PitchMind Scoring

The striker operates in the highest-value zones of the pitch (final third, penalty area) and touches the ball in moments with the highest outcome variance — finishing chances, link-up under pressure, first touches in tight spaces. This means:

- Decision quality has maximum match impact
- Technical execution mistakes (poor first touch, missed finish) are immediately visible and consequential
- The scorer must weight errors higher here than in deeper positions
- Off-ball intelligence (movement, positioning, runs) is frequent and high-value, even when the player never receives the ball in a given sequence

## 2. KEY RESPONSIBILITIES

### 2A. Attacking Phase Responsibilities

**Finishing:** Converting goal-scoring opportunities across all shot types — composed 1v1s, placed efforts from outside the box, headers, volleys, tap-ins, penalty kicks. The striker is the terminal action of most attacking sequences.

**Hold-Up Play:** Receiving the ball with back to goal under defensive pressure. Shielding the ball, buying seconds for midfielders to arrive, and then laying off, turning, or drawing a foul. This is a fundamental skill in youth/college football where direct long balls are common.

**Combination Play in the Final Third:** Third-man runs, wall passes (1-2s), overlapping runs from wide players, underlap invitations. The striker must be a willing and intelligent short-range combination partner.

**Creating Space for Others:** The striker's movement — even without the ball — is an attacking action. A correctly timed run in behind occupies two center-backs and creates room for a #10 to receive facing goal.

**Pressing from the Front:** Initiating the team's defensive shape when out of possession. The striker is the first defensive line and sets the angle/pressure that makes the press system work.

**Second-Ball Recovery:** After long balls, headers, or goalkeeper distribution, the striker must anticipate where the ball will drop and arrive first or contest effectively.

### 2B. Defensive Phase Responsibilities

**Pressing Initiation:** The striker determines when and how the team presses. Pressing too early, too late, or from the wrong angle collapses the structure behind. A well-triggered press forces the opposition into errors in their own half.

**Blocking Passing Lanes:** By careful positioning of the body and angling the press, the striker can simultaneously apply pressure and close off the opposition's most dangerous distribution option (typically the #6 or #8 receiving centrally).

**Disrupting Build-Up:** Pressing the goalkeeper or center-backs when the trigger exists (bad touch, back pass under pressure, no available outlet), forcing rushed clearances or turnovers.

**Tracking Runs Defensively:** When the opposition inverts their build-up or switches play, the striker must adjust to stay goalside and restrict progression. High-energy positional discipline maintains the team's defensive shape.

## 3. MOVEMENT PATTERNS AND POSITIONING PRINCIPLES

### 3A. Core Movement Vocabulary

**The Channel Run:** A diagonal sprint into the space between center-back and fullback (the "channel"). This is the most dangerous run in football. It stretches the defense horizontally, forces the center-back to track or leave their zone, and creates a clear shooting/crossing scenario. Triggered by: a teammate with time on the ball in a wide or central area, a high defensive line, or transition moments.

**The Blindside Run:** Moving into a defender's blind spot — typically behind and to the side — so the defender must choose between tracking the run (and vacating their zone) or ignoring it (allowing a free run). Classic in crosses, set pieces, and diagonal balls.

**The Spin Off:** Checking toward the ball (as if to receive to feet), then spinning the opposite direction once the ball is played — using the initial movement to lose the marker. Requires high spatial awareness and precise timing.

**The Drop-and-Turn:** Dropping toward the ball, receiving to feet, and using a first touch or turn to face goal. Used when the defense is set deep and there's no channel to run into. A classic hold-up skill.

**The False 9 Drift:** Drifting wide or into the half-space to receive between the lines, drawing center-backs out of position and creating a gap for wide runners to exploit in behind.

**Penalty Area Positioning:** The striker should constantly adjust position relative to the ball's location, the ball carrier's body orientation, and the defensive shape. General principles:
- Near-post run when the ball is wide and deep
- Far-post positioning when the ball is central and the cross is likely to be pulled back
- Penalty spot occupation when the wide player is likely to cross early
- Movement away from the ball immediately before it's delivered (creates a yard of separation)

### 3B. Off-Ball Positioning Principles

**The Second-Post Principle:** When a teammate takes a shot, the striker must immediately move toward the second post — not the first, not the center. The majority of rebounds travel toward the far post due to the goalkeeper's angle. This is observable, specific, and reflects elite positional awareness.

**Staggering Runs with Wide Players:** The striker must not make the same run as the wide player. If the LW cuts inside, the ST should run in behind on the opposite side to stretch the defense in two directions simultaneously.

**Occupying Two Defenders:** An elite striker who positions themselves in the zone between two center-backs forces both to make a decision. Neither can ignore the striker without conceding the run. This 1v2 occupancy is a high-value off-ball action.

**Line-Breaking Positioning:** Before the ball is played, the striker should position their body and make subtle movements to ensure they are between the defensive line and the goal — "on the line" — ready to break behind in a single step when the through-ball is played.

## 4. DECISION-MAKING FRAMEWORK

### 4A. Decision Tree: Receiving the Ball

When the striker is about to receive the ball, the decision flow is:

1. **Scan Before Receipt:** Before the ball arrives, scan both shoulders to identify pressure (distance and direction), available teammates, space behind the defender, and goal location. Body orientation upon receipt determines what options are open.

2. **Facing Goal?**
   - YES → First touch toward goal or cut into shooting lane. Evaluate: shoot, dribble at goal, or play quickly to avoid pressure.
   - NO (back to goal) → Is there time to turn?
     - YES → Turn with first touch (away from pressure)
     - NO → Hold, lay off, or draw foul. Who is arriving in support?

3. **Under Pressure?**
   - Pressure within 1 yard → First touch away from pressure, lay off or spin
   - Pressure within 2-3 yards → Touch across, use body to shield, evaluate turn
   - Space available → Touch into space, drive forward, take on or shoot

4. **Is There a Shooting Angle?**
   - Clean angle, goalkeeper exposed → Shoot low, far post
   - Partial angle → Cut inside or plant shot to near post
   - No angle → Set back, continue the attack

### 4B. Decision Tree: When to Run In Behind

**Run in behind when:**
- A teammate has the ball at their feet with head up and space to release
- The defensive line is high (above the opponent's midfield line)
- There has been a turnover and the defense is transitioning
- A wide player has received and is cutting inside (striker makes the opposite run)
- The goalkeeper is up/aggressive — there may be space behind

**Do NOT run in behind when:**
- The ball carrier is under heavy pressure (run will be wasted, likely offside anyway)
- The defensive line is deep and compact (there is no space to run into)
- You have already made two unsuccessful runs in quick succession — rest, reset position
- The team is in a build-up phase and you're needed as a short combination option

### 4C. Decision Tree: When to Press

**Press when (triggers):**
- Opposition center-back receives a back pass and has their back to goal
- The ball is played to the goalkeeper with their back to the goal
- A lateral/backward pass is made with the receiver's first touch going away from goal
- The opposition fullback receives with no immediate forward option
- A teammate is already pressing the nearby player (press the next pass option, not the ball)

**Do NOT press when:**
- The opposition has multiple short options available (press will be bypassed)
- You are isolated with no support behind — pressing alone cedes the space behind
- The team is defending deep in a low block (pressing from the front is contradictory)
- You have just made an attacking run and have low energy — press trigger should go to a teammate

### 4D. Hold-Up vs. Turn Decision

This is one of the most frequently scored decisions for a striker. The framework:

**Turn when:**
- Pressure is not immediate (defender is 2+ yards away)
- There is space in front to exploit
- The first touch creates separation

**Hold when:**
- Defender is tight on the back (body pressure felt)
- Turning would result in losing possession
- A teammate is making a run or arriving in support
- A foul can be drawn by absorbing contact

**Lay Off when:**
- Holding is not sustainable
- A midfielder has arrived in the correct support position
- A third-man combination is available

## 5. TECHNICAL REQUIREMENTS

### 5A. First Touch

The striker's first touch is the most consequential single technical act in the position. A striker who routinely controls the ball cleanly with an excellent first touch:
- Creates time on the ball (even from tight service)
- Maintains attacking momentum
- Reduces defensive recovery time

**Observable quality markers:**
- Ball stayed within 1 yard of body on receipt under moderate pressure
- First touch angled to create space or prepare the next action
- Touch that turns the body toward goal simultaneously
- Cushion touch on aerial balls (doesn't bounce away)

**Types of first touch the striker must master:**
1. **Chest-and-turn:** Aerial ball, chest down, touch around the defender
2. **Side-foot cushion:** Pace on a through-ball taken on the side foot, directing into space
3. **Instep kill:** Killing the ball under the foot — used in tight spaces
4. **Flick-on touch:** Redirecting long aerial balls in behind, using momentum rather than stopping it
5. **Thigh trap:** Medium-height balls, thigh used to kill and direct
6. **One-touch redirect:** Ball played at pace, first touch becomes the finish

### 5B. Finishing

Finishing is the most visible, high-pressure technical skill in football. For PitchMind scoring, the focus is on **decision quality** inside the technical execution — did the player choose the right finish for the situation?

**Placement over power:** In most 1v1 and near-post situations, placement into the corner is higher probability than a power shot. An elite striker consistently makes this decision.

**Shot selection framework:**
- **Goalkeeper coming out (1v1):** Slot to the far post along the ground, or chip if GK is very committed
- **Angle shot (side of area):** Near post at pace — hardest for GK to reach from angle
- **Central, inside box:** Far post placement or low driven to GK's weak side
- **Outside box:** Low, driven, with power — bounce shots troublesome for keepers
- **Aerial (header):** Downward, near or far post, toward the corner — never straight at GK
- **Volley:** Technique over power — body over the ball, strike through the center

**Rebound anticipation:** After saving shots, the goalkeeper's position telegraphs where the rebound will go. An elite striker reads this and moves before the ball comes back.

### 5C. Distribution / Link-Up Passing

The striker is not primarily a distribution player, but clean, accurate passing under pressure is essential:

**Key passes for a striker:**
- **Lay-off:** Back to the ball under pressure — one or two-touch, directed away from pressure into the path of arriving midfielders
- **Flick-on:** Using the first touch to redirect a through-pass or long ball around a defender
- **Wall pass / 1-2:** Playing into a wide player and immediately sprinting past the defender to receive back
- **Third-man combination:** Playing a short pass while a third player makes the diagonal run to receive the return pass in behind the defense

**What to avoid:**
- Holding the ball too long in the build-up phase
- Playing back under pressure when a forward option exists
- Overcomplicating in the final third — fewer touches often = better outcomes

## 6. TACTICAL AWARENESS: READING THE GAME AND TRANSITIONS

### 6A. Reading Defensive Shape

An elite striker reads the defensive shape before they make their run. Key reads:

**High line:** Run in behind immediately — the space is there
**Flat back four with no cover shadow:** Attack the channel diagonally
**Center-backs split wide:** Attack the center gap on the ground
**Center-backs tight and central:** Run away from the ball, pull one CB, create space for a winger
**Zonal marking in the box:** Target the gap between two zones, arrive just as the ball does
**Man-marking:** Use movement to lose your marker — spin, check, spin again

### 6B. Transition to Attack (Positive Transition)

When the team wins the ball, the striker has 2-3 seconds to make the highest-impact decision. This is the most valuable attacking moment in football:

**What elite strikers do:**
- If they have the ball: sprint forward immediately, drive at the space between the defensive lines
- If a teammate has the ball: immediately run in behind the disorganized defense — don't wait to see what happens
- Read the defensive recovery: if the center-back is running forward/sideways, they are not goal-side — exploit it
- Read the ball carrier: if the carrier has head up and vision, make the run; if the carrier is under pressure, hold nearby as a reset option

### 6C. Transition to Defense (Negative Transition)

When the team loses the ball:

**The first 3 seconds:** The striker becomes the first line of press. If the turnover is in the final third, immediately press the center-back who received — there is no time to retreat.

**Beyond 3 seconds:** The team has transitioned into a defensive shape. The striker:
- Adopts an angled press from above the opposition's half
- Blocks central passing lanes to prevent progressive passes
- Does NOT sprint back into their own half — they are the first defensive wall, not a defender

### 6D. Pressing Triggers (Observable Cues)

Leo must observe and score pressing decisions against these triggers:

| Trigger | Correct Striker Response |
|---------|--------------------------|
| Back pass to GK | Sprint to pressure GK |
| CB receives, back to goal | Press from angle covering most dangerous pass |
| Lateral pass with poor first touch | Press hard, teammate covers next option |
| GK has ball, no immediate outlet | Press wide, force long ball |
| Defensive midfielder receives under pressure | Hold position, prevent return pass |

## 7. RADAR AXES FOR STRIKER

The PitchMind radar for the ST position uses 7 axes, each reflecting a distinct dimension of performance:

### Axis 1: Finishing Quality
**Justification:** The primary output of the position. Measures shot selection accuracy, placement decision under pressure, and conversion efficiency. An imprecise first touch or poor shot choice in scoring positions is a weighted error for ST more than any other outfield position.

### Axis 2: Movement Intelligence
**Justification:** Captures the quality of off-ball runs — channel runs, blindside movements, penalty box positioning, and run timing relative to the ball carrier's readiness to release. High-value actions that don't touch the ball but determine team success.

### Axis 3: First Touch (Receiving Quality)
**Justification:** The striker receives in the highest-pressure areas with the least time. A clean first touch either creates or destroys an attacking opportunity. This axis is weighted more heavily for ST than for any other position — a poor touch in the box is extremely penalized.

### Axis 4: Hold-Up and Link-Up
**Justification:** Measures the ability to receive, shield, and distribute effectively under physical pressure. Especially critical for youth/college environments where long balls are common and the striker must be a reliable retention player.

### Axis 5: Press Initiation Quality
**Justification:** How well the striker triggers and executes their defensive pressing role. Correct angles, reading the trigger, and the timing of the press. An incorrectly angled press that allows a switch or a central pass is a scored error.

### Axis 6: Transition Exploitation
**Justification:** Captures the striker's activity in the 3-second window after a turnover. A striker who immediately makes the correct run and creates a dangerous transition moment scores high here; a striker who stands still or runs the wrong direction scores low.

### Axis 7: Decision Under Pressure
**Justification:** A composite of hold-vs-turn decisions, pass selection in tight areas, and composure in 1v1 situations. Measures whether the player's decisions under time/space/physical pressure match the optimal choice for the scenario.

## 8. SUB-CATEGORY SCORING RELEVANCE FOR ST

### Probability Sub-Categories (Soccer IQ Layer)

**Decision Making (HIGHEST WEIGHT for ST)**
The striker encounters more binary, high-consequence decisions per match than almost any other player: shoot or pass, run in behind or hold, press or drop. Leo should flag and score every one of these decision moments. Poor decision making in the final third (e.g., choosing to shoot when the goalkeeper has already committed vs. passing to an open teammate) is the primary differentiator between average and elite strikers.

**Positioning (HIGH WEIGHT)**
The striker's positioning off the ball determines what options the team can access. Penalty box positioning, run-line selection, and starting position before a sequence begins are all scored here. A striker who is always in a dangerous position without the ball — even if they never touch it — demonstrates elite positioning.

**Reading the Game (HIGH WEIGHT)**
The striker must anticipate: when the ball will be played into the channel, when the press trigger will emerge, when the team needs a hold-up player vs. a run option. Reading the game separates the reactive striker from the proactive one. Anticipatory runs that arrive perfectly timed (even if the pass is never played) are scored positively.

**Awareness (MODERATE-HIGH WEIGHT)**
Scanning before receiving, identifying cover pressure, knowing where the goalkeeper is and where teammates are available. A striker who receives the ball without scanning and then makes a predictable play scores lower.

**Concentration/Focus (MODERATE WEIGHT)**
Strikers who lose focus between touches — especially during sustained opponent possession — miss second balls, fail to press triggers, and lose their defensive positioning discipline. Observable moments: ignoring a loose ball in their zone, not tracking a runner who becomes dangerous.

**Problem-Solving (MODERATE WEIGHT)**
When the planned option disappears (pass blocked, run cut off, space closed), the elite striker finds the next solution quickly. Dragging a ball down and making a quick turn when a long ball unexpectedly arrives, or drawing a foul when no clean option exists — these are scored as problem-solving actions.

### Execution Sub-Categories (Technical Layer)

**Shooting (HIGHEST WEIGHT for ST)**
Every shooting action is scored. Decision (placement vs. power, angle choice, timing) is the primary score, with technique (contact, lean, follow-through) as secondary. A technically well-struck shot to the wrong location still scores lower than a correctly placed but less perfectly struck effort.

**First Touch (HIGHEST WEIGHT for ST)**
Every first touch in the final third is evaluated. Touches that maintain possession and create time/space score high. Touches that bounce away, go out of play, or give away possession score low. Context matters: a difficult aerial service that is reasonably controlled scores differently than a simple side-foot pass that is fumbled.

**Distribution (MODERATE WEIGHT for ST)**
Lay-offs, combination passes, flick-ons — the quality and accuracy of all outgoing passes are evaluated. A striker who consistently puts their combination partners in good positions with clean lay-offs demonstrates high distribution quality.

**Shielding (MODERATE WEIGHT for ST)**
Back-to-goal situations are scored on the quality of the shield: body position (between ball and defender), use of arms/width, balance, and the ability to absorb pressure. Poor shielding that results in a turnover is a weighted negative.

**Dribbling (LOWER-MODERATE WEIGHT for ST)**
One-on-one situations in and around the box, beating a press, driving into channels — scored but weighted less than shooting and first touch. Dribbling decisions (when to commit to carrying vs. passing) are part of the Decision Making score.

**Tackling (MINIMAL WEIGHT for ST)**
Strikers are rarely evaluated on tackling. Pressing actions are separate (Probability layer). If a striker makes a tackle, it's evaluated on technique, but it's not a primary axis.

## 9. COMMON MISTAKES AND HOW ELITE PLAYERS AVOID THEM

### Mistake 1: The Static Wait
**Description:** The striker stands in one position for multiple seconds during sustained possession phases, waiting for the ball to come to them. They are not scanning, not repositioning, not making subtle movements.
**Why it happens:** Fatigue, low concentration, over-reliance on the ball being forced to them.
**Elite correction:** Constant micro-movement — 2-3 steps to change angle, adjust spacing from the defender, create a new passing lane. Even when no pass is coming, movement maintains threat and concentration.

### Mistake 2: Running Offside
**Description:** Making the channel run too early — before the ball carrier has looked up or before the through-ball window is open. Results in the run being wasted and the player in an offside position.
**Why it happens:** Excitement/anticipation, not reading the ball carrier's body language.
**Elite correction:** Learn to read the passer's body orientation. The run in behind starts one second *after* the ball carrier opens their hips toward you and lifts their head. Timing is everything — a late run that arrives on time beats an early run that arrives offside.

### Mistake 3: The Wrong-Foot First Touch
**Description:** Receiving a ball coming from the left, but taking the first touch with the right foot (the wrong foot for the scenario) — resulting in the body being turned away from goal, or a bounced touch.
**Why it happens:** Lack of pre-scanning (not knowing where the ball and defender are before receipt), poor body orientation.
**Elite correction:** Pre-scan before the ball arrives. Orient the body toward the ball with the receiving foot predetermined. The first touch should already know where it's going before the ball makes contact.

### Mistake 4: Telegraphed Runs
**Description:** Striker always runs the same channel (typically the near-side channel), making their run readable and easily tracked.
**Why it happens:** Habitual movement, limited movement vocabulary.
**Elite correction:** Vary run types and timing within every match. Inside-to-out, out-to-in, drop-and-spin. Create unpredictability. If a defender is reading every run, change the run immediately.

### Mistake 5: Pressing Blind
**Description:** Sprinting straight at the ball carrier without closing the nearest passing lane first.
**Why it happens:** High energy, desire to win the ball, lack of tactical pressing education.
**Elite correction:** Approach at an angle — press the ball carrier while cutting off the easiest/most dangerous outlet. The angle of press is more important than the speed of press.

### Mistake 6: Taking Too Many Touches Before Shooting
**Description:** In a clear shooting position, the striker takes 2-3 unnecessary touches — allowing the goalkeeper to recover, defenders to close, or the angle to disappear.
**Why it happens:** Lack of composure, anxiety about the finish, not recognizing the window.
**Elite correction:** If the shooting angle is clean, the first touch IS the shot. One-touch finishing in the box is a skill that must be drilled so it becomes automatic. The more touches, the more the defense closes.

### Mistake 7: Ball-Watching During Crosses
**Description:** Standing still watching the ball be crossed rather than moving into the optimal delivery zone.
**Why it happens:** Uncertainty about where the cross will land, ball-watching instinct.
**Elite correction:** The cross zone (near, far, penalty spot) is determined BEFORE the ball is delivered, based on the crosser's approach angle and body position. Begin the movement before the ball is struck.

## 10. AGE-GROUP CONSIDERATIONS

### U12 (Ages 10-12)
**Primary focus:** Fundamental movement patterns, basic finishing technique, first touch development.
**What Leo scores at this level:**
- Was the first touch in the right direction?
- Did the player try to get into a shooting position?
- Did the player move off the ball at all?
**What Leo does NOT penalize heavily:**
- Complex pressing decisions
- Incorrect hold-up decisions under high pressure
- Tactical run selection nuance

**Key behaviors to reward:** Any forward movement, any attempt to get on the back shoulder, any effort to face goal before receiving.

### U16 (Ages 14-16)
**Primary focus:** Decision-making speed, pressing triggers, hold-up vs. turn decisions, channel run timing.
**What Leo scores:**
- Hold-up decision quality (turn vs. lay off)
- Pressing angles and trigger recognition
- Run selection (in behind vs. combine)
- First touch quality under moderate pressure
**Expectations:** Players should be demonstrating positional awareness, scanning before receipt, and varied movement vocabulary.

### U18 (Ages 16-18)
**Primary focus:** Full tactical awareness, 1v1 finishing composure, transition exploitation, combination play.
**What Leo scores:**
- All seven radar axes at near-full weight
- Transition run timing in the first 3 seconds
- Body orientation on receipt
- Finish selection in 1v1 and inside-box scenarios
**Expectations:** Elite-level movement vocabulary expected. Poor decisions in clear shooting positions are heavily penalized. Pressing angles should match team system.

### College (Ages 18-22)
**Primary focus:** All professional-standard demands applied. Consistency, repeatability, tactical discipline.
**What Leo scores:**
- Full radar at maximum weight
- Decisions in high-pressure moments (shot selection, hold-up under aggressive defending)
- Contribution to team pressing structure
- Transition exploitation within the first 2 seconds
**Expectations:** Every fundamental should be automatic. Errors here represent real deficiencies, not development gaps. Comparison benchmarks shift to professional youth standards.

## 11. ELITE PLAYER EXAMPLES (BEHAVIORAL PATTERNS)

### Player Type A: The Intelligent Mover
**Pattern:** Never stops moving off the ball. Before every ball is played into the forward line, this player has already made 2-3 preparatory movements — checking away, stepping toward the ball, then spinning in behind. These micro-movements create separation that looks effortless.
**Key behavior:** Reads the center-back's eyes. The moment the CB glances elsewhere (toward the ball, at a teammate), the run begins.
**Scoring relevance:** Consistently high on Movement Intelligence and Transition Exploitation axes.

### Player Type B: The Technical Finisher
**Pattern:** Rarely flashy in possession, but in the final yards their touch and finish are near-automatic. First touch in the box always creates a shooting angle. Shot choice is calm, placed rather than powered. Favors the far corner at low height.
**Key behavior:** Scans both GK position and the nearest defender before receiving in the box. First touch always takes them away from the nearest defender.
**Scoring relevance:** Consistently high on Finishing Quality and First Touch axes.

### Player Type C: The Hold-Up Anchor
**Pattern:** Back to goal, absorbs pressure, always finds the lay-off. Never turns into pressure. Uses body width to shield and always plays the safe option — but plays it quickly. Creates rhythm for midfielders arriving behind.
**Key behavior:** Arms wide, low center of gravity. Receives contact, stays upright. One-touch lay-off to the side, never back through the middle.
**Scoring relevance:** High on Hold-Up and Link-Up and Distribution axes. Acceptable on Finishing Quality.

### Player Type D: The Channel Exploiter
**Pattern:** Spends most of the match in wide channels, dragging center-backs out of position. When the ball is switched wide, immediately runs the far channel. Arrival in the box is often as a second attacker on the cross.
**Key behavior:** Times the run to arrive as the cross is struck — not before (offside risk) and not after (defender has recovered). Uses momentum to attack the ball in the air.
**Scoring relevance:** High on Movement Intelligence and Transition Exploitation. Medium on Finishing Quality.

### Player Type E: The Press Leader
**Pattern:** Dictates the team's defensive shape from the front. Every press has a specific angle — opens one side and forces the CB to play wide. Teammates read the press direction and cover accordingly. Even when the press doesn't win the ball, it forces a long ball.
**Key behavior:** Approaches at 45°, shoulder toward the dangerous outlet, body angle forces ball wide. Never sprints directly at the ball carrier.
**Scoring relevance:** High on Press Initiation Quality and Decision Under Pressure.

## 12. COACHING CUES AND KEYWORDS

**Movement Keywords:**
- "Channel early, arrive late" — make the run early but time arrival on the pass
- "Check and spin" — step toward the ball, then spin away from the defender
- "Second post first" — on any shot, move to second post for rebounds
- "Read the hips" — the ball carrier's hip orientation tells you when to run

**Finishing Keywords:**
- "Near post line, far post mind" — approach near post but be prepared to redirect far
- "Hit the keeper's weak side" — if the GK is a right-foot keeper, finish to their left
- "First touch = the shot" — in the box, the first touch should be the decision
- "Low = harder to save" — ground shots convert more than height shots at all youth levels

**Pressing Keywords:**
- "Angle is everything" — the press direction matters more than the speed
- "Close the easy ball" — stand between the ball carrier and their nearest outlet
- "Trigger, don't chase" — wait for the trigger, then commit fully

**Hold-Up Keywords:**
- "Wide body, low center" — arms out, hips low to shield
- "Find the lay-off before you receive" — know where the ball is going before it arrives
- "One touch, move" — lay it off and immediately move into combination space

## 13. TRAINING EXERCISES

### Exercise 1: Finishing Circuits (First Touch → Finish)
**Purpose:** Develops the connection between first touch quality and shot selection.
**Setup:** Coach or server delivers balls from various angles to a striker on the edge of the box. Each delivery requires a different first touch — cushion to the side, forward, across — followed immediately by a finish.
**Leo relevance:** Trains the First Touch and Finishing Quality axes simultaneously. Observe: does the first touch create a shooting opportunity, or does it require an extra touch?

### Exercise 2: Hold-Up and Combine (3v2)
**Purpose:** Develops hold-up play and combination decisions.
**Setup:** Two attacking midfielders + 1 striker vs. 2 defenders in a 25x20 zone. Long ball played to striker under pressure. Striker must decide: hold, turn, lay off, or combine. Score a point for clean lay-offs that result in the attacker playing forward.
**Leo relevance:** Trains Hold-Up/Link-Up, Distribution, and Decision Under Pressure axes.

### Exercise 3: Channel Runs with Timing Gates
**Purpose:** Develops run timing relative to ball carrier readiness.
**Setup:** Ball carrier at 25 yards, striker in central position. Ball carrier has a "green light" signal (raise head, open hips). Striker must begin channel run only on the green light. After 20 reps, add an offsides trigger for early runs.
**Leo relevance:** Trains Movement Intelligence and Transition Exploitation.

### Exercise 4: Pressing Angles Drill
**Purpose:** Develops correct angled pressing approach.
**Setup:** Striker presses a CB mannequin from 20 yards. On approach, must arrive at 45° angle, right shoulder forward to force play right. Coach gives verbal trigger (drop pass, bad touch), striker initiates from a standstill.
**Leo relevance:** Trains Press Initiation Quality.

### Exercise 5: 1v1 Finishing Composure
**Purpose:** Develops composure and decision quality in 1v1 scenarios.
**Setup:** Ball played in behind the defense, striker vs. goalkeeper. Vary the delivery distance: 10, 20, 30 yards in behind. Goalkeeper can come or hold. Striker must correctly read GK position and select the finish (slot, chip, driven).
**Leo relevance:** Trains Finishing Quality and Decision Under Pressure. Specifically develops composure under time pressure.

### Exercise 6: Transition Exploitation Game
**Purpose:** Develops first-action speed in transition.
**Setup:** Possession game in the midfield. When a turnover is made, the team that wins the ball has 5 seconds to create a shooting chance. The striker must immediately make the correct decision — run or receive — in the first 2 seconds.
**Leo relevance:** Trains Transition Exploitation axis and Decision Making sub-category.

### Exercise 7: Scanning and Receiving (Back-to-Goal)
**Purpose:** Builds pre-receipt scanning habit.
**Setup:** Striker faces away from goal. Coach holds up a colored bib. Before the ball is played to their feet, striker must call out the color (proving they have scanned). Then receive, decide (turn or lay off), execute.
**Leo relevance:** Trains Awareness sub-category and First Touch axis. Forces pre-receipt information gathering.
