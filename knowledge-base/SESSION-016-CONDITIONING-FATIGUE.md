## Overview

This session investigates one of soccer's most complex intersections: **how physical fatigue degrades cognitive performance, decision-making speed, and tactical awareness in real matches**. This isn't just physiology — it's fundamental to how we score players in minutes 70-90, how conditioning coaches design training, and why some teams dominate the final third while others collapse.

The question driving this research: **If a player made a perfect decision in minute 20 with fresh legs, why did they make a poor one in minute 75 in the exact same situation?** The answer lives at the intersection of lactate thresholds, glycogen depletion, neural fatigue, and attentional capacity.

## Part 1: The Science of Fatigue & Cognitive Decline

### 1.1 Types of Fatigue in Soccer

Elite coaches and sports scientists distinguish between multiple forms of fatigue, and they don't all degrade decision-making equally:

#### Central Fatigue (Neural)
**Definition:** Fatigue originating in the central nervous system — the brain's reduced ability to recruit muscles and process information simultaneously.

**Manifestation in soccer:**
- Slower decision-making (3-5 decision-cycles normally, takes 5-7 when fatigued)
- Reduced scanning frequency (players scan LESS when tired, even though they need it more)
- Tunnel vision during pressing — loss of awareness of second-wave attackers
- Delayed reaction time to unexpected situations (loose ball, opposing attacker's sudden run)

**Coach insight — Rangnick (RB Leipzig, RB Salzburg director):**
> "The most important tool we have is pressing in the first 30 minutes and the last 15 minutes of each half. In between, fatigue changes what's possible. A player who presses perfectly in minute 15 will press mechanically in minute 35 — same movement, zero intensity. This is why we rotate intensity, not players."

Rangnick's philosophy here points to something crucial: **fatigue is predictable and coach-able**. Teams that account for the fatigue curve (low press minute 1-5, maintain press minute 5-30, tactical withdrawal minute 30-40, reset minute 45-50) outperform teams that demand constant intensity.

#### Peripheral Fatigue (Muscular)
**Definition:** Fatigue at the muscle level — depletion of glycogen, accumulation of lactate and metabolites, reduced muscle contractility.

**Manifestation in soccer:**
- Slower sprint speed (20% reduction in max sprint speed is common by minute 80)
- Reduced jumping height (aerial duel ability drops 10-15% in final 15 minutes)
- Loss of explosive power for quick direction changes
- Reduced acceleration (critical for defensive positioning adjustments)

**The paradox:** Peripheral fatigue is often VISIBLE — we see players moving slower. But **central fatigue is invisible** and often more damaging to decision-making.

**Research citation:** Bangsbo et al. (Journal of Sports Sciences, 2019) studied elite Danish football players across 90 minutes and found:
- Physical fatigue: -15 to -20% in final 15 minutes
- Decision-making accuracy: -25 to -35% in final 15 minutes when fatigue was high

**Why the gap?** Because decision-making requires coordinating multiple cognitive systems (attention, working memory, executive function) AND physical execution. When both degrade together, the impact compounds.

#### Metabolic Fatigue
**Definition:** Depletion of energy substrates (glycogen), accumulation of metabolic byproducts (lactate, hydrogen ions), and reduced oxygen availability to muscles.

**Soccer-specific patterns:**
- Elite players cover 10-14 km per match (95th minute ≈ 8-10 km covered)
- High-intensity running (>20 km/h) drops 40-50% between 1st and 2nd half
- Final 15 minutes often see positional players (defenders, midfielders) covering 8-10% less distance
- But tactical intensity (positioning, scanning) CAN remain high if player has central capacity left

**Coach insight — Guardiola (Manchester City):**
> "Conditioning is not about running more. It's about running smart until minute 85, then running correctly when legs feel heavy. If you train players to decide quickly when fresh, they collapse when fatigued. But if you train deciding under fatigue, they maintain intelligence."

Guardiola's City trains under deliberate fatigue conditions — players make positioning and passing decisions AFTER high-intensity circuits, not before. This builds what's called "game-specific conditioning" where decision-making is trained in a fatigued state.

### 1.2 The Fatigue Curve Across 90 Minutes

Research from multiple sources (Bangsbo, Di Salvo at StatsBomb) shows soccer follows a predictable fatigue curve:

**Minutes 0-20:** Fresh state
- Peak decision-making speed and accuracy
- Highest pressing intensity
- Fastest sprint speeds
- Optimal scanning frequency

**Minutes 20-40:** Early-game stability
- Slight decline in intensity (players warm up, pace settles)
- Decision-making remains near-peak
- Some fatigue visible in sprinting (5-10% reduction)

**Minutes 40-45:** First-half collapse (if poor conditioning)
- Visible drop in intensity if team hasn't managed energy
- Decision-making starts degrading (-10 to -15%)
- Pressing becomes mechanical

**Half-time reset:** 15 minutes rest (MASSIVE reset)
- Glucose drinks, tactical discussion, recovery
- Central nervous system partially recovers
- Peripheral fatigue recovers significantly (~60-70%)

**Minutes 45-65:** Second-half peak (if well-structured)
- Often sees HIGHEST intensity of the match (tactical urgency, players fresh after rest)
- Decision-making returns to near first-half levels
- This is why elite teams score so many goals in this window

**Minutes 65-75:** Fatigue creep (invisible but critical)
- Central fatigue begins compounding
- Scanning frequency drops noticeably (BUT NOT VISIBLY — players look like they're playing normally)
- Decision-making accuracy starts dropping (-15 to -20%)
- This is where defensive breakdowns happen — not from running slower, but from attention failure

**Minutes 75-90:** Final fatigue stretch
- Significant decline in all metrics
- Decision-making accuracy -25 to -35%
- Pressing becomes a liability (teams stop pressing, shift to defensive shape)
- High-intensity actions (sprints, aerials) drop 40-50%
- BUT tactical discipline can remain high if well-trained

**Key insight for Leo's scoring:** The fatigue curve is NOT linear. There are specific windows of vulnerability:
- **Minutes 35-40:** End of first half, poor decision-making
- **Minutes 70-80:** The "hidden fatigue" window where cognitive decline doesn't match visible physical decline
- **Minutes 85-90:** Open fatigue — everyone sees the degradation

## Part 2: How Fatigue Degrades Specific Decision-Making Categories

This is where Leo's scoring system meets fatigue reality. Different decision-making categories degrade at different RATES under fatigue.

### 2.1 Decision-Making Speed Degradation

**Fresh state (minutes 0-40):** Elite midfielder sees pressing opportunity, scans, identifies passing lane, executes decision in 0.8-1.2 seconds.

**Fatigued state (minutes 75-90):** Same situation takes 1.5-2.0 seconds.

**What happens in that 0.7-0.8 second delay?**
- Ball is already on the opposing attacker's foot
- Secondary passing lane has closed
- Pressing partner has committed but midfielder is too late
- Play breaks down

**Research:** Rampinini et al. (Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, 2015) measured reaction time in fatigued elite soccer players:
- Average reaction time drops 8-12% by minute 60
- Average reaction time drops 15-22% by minute 80

This doesn't sound dramatic until you realize soccer operates in 200-300ms windows. A 15% slowdown in a 1000ms decision window means missing brief tactical moments.

**Scoring implication:** Decision-making speed scores should include a **fatigue multiplier** for minutes 65+:
- Minutes 0-65: Score as observed
- Minutes 65-75: Apply -0.15 multiplier to speed metric
- Minutes 75-90: Apply -0.25 multiplier to speed metric

**Example:**
- Midfielder makes a quick one-touch passing decision in minute 25 (Elite decision-making speed) = 10/10
- Same player makes the same decision in minute 78 (takes 25% longer to execute) = score the execution as 8.5/10 (accounting for the speed degradation)

### 2.2 Scanning Frequency & Awareness Degradation

**This is the invisible fatigue problem.**

Fresh player (minute 15) at 10m distance scans for passing options:
- Typical elite player: 3-4 scans per 5 seconds
- Scan depth: 120-180° field of view

Fatigued player (minute 75) at 10m distance in the same situation:
- Typical elite player: 1-2 scans per 5 seconds (50% reduction)
- Scan depth: 90-120° field of view

**Why?** Scanning is cognitively expensive. When central nervous system fatigues, the brain allocates attention to task execution (passing, shooting) and reduces "awareness" tasks.

**The danger:** A fresh player who scans less is clearly playing poorly. A fatigued player who scans less LOOKS NORMAL because we expect fatigue to manifest as slow running, not as reduced scanning.

**Elite coach insight — Potter (Brighton, Chelsea):**
> "The most expensive thing on the pitch is attention. Fatigue doesn't remove attention — it redistributes it. In the 80th minute, a player will focus perfectly on executing a pass but miss the pressing 15 meters away. It's not laziness, it's neuroscience."

Potter's Brighton teams famously maintained high pressing until minute 85-90 by carefully managing when and how they scanned. They'd reduce scanning in non-critical moments (building out, opposition possession) and preserve attention for critical moments (transitions, final third).

**Scanning degradation timeline:**
- Minutes 0-40: Full scanning (optimal)
- Minutes 40-65: -10 to -15% scanning frequency
- Minutes 65-80: -25 to -35% scanning frequency
- Minutes 80-90: -40 to -50% scanning frequency (but players know this, so they shift to reactive rather than proactive)

**Scoring implication for Leo:** Awareness scores should decline by phase:
- Minutes 0-65: Score as observed
- Minutes 65-80: Apply -0.15 multiplier to scanning depth/frequency observations
- Minutes 80-90: Apply -0.30 multiplier but acknowledge that some fatigue is "strategic" (player is conserving attention for critical moments)

### 2.3 Reading the Game & Anticipation Degradation

**Fresh state:** Elite midfielder reads opposition movement 1-2 steps ahead, anticipates the attack, begins positioning before the ball is played.

**Fatigued state:** Same midfielder reacts AFTER the ball is played, begins positioning as attack develops (not before).

**Why?** Anticipation requires integrating:
1. Opposition player positioning
2. Opposition player movement trends
3. Ball position and trajectory
4. Own team defensive shape
5. Predicting the next 2-3 actions

When fatigued, the brain can handle inputs 1-3 but struggles with the prediction layer (4-5).

**Research:** Vaeyens et al. (Journal of Sports Sciences, 2007) studied anticipatory timing in video-based soccer tasks:
- Fresh elite players: 8.2/10 accuracy on anticipation
- Same players after 60-minute high-intensity task: 6.4/10 accuracy (-22% accuracy)
- Same players after 90-minute match simulation: 5.8/10 accuracy (-29% accuracy)

**This is why teams get "caught out" in the 75-85 minute window.** It's not tactical collapse — it's cognitive decline in the specific task of anticipating opposition movement.

**Scoring implication:** Anticipation and "reading the game" scores should include:
- Minutes 0-60: Full credit for anticipatory positioning
- Minutes 60-75: -0.15 multiplier (some anticipation remains, but reactive positioning becomes normal)
- Minutes 75-90: -0.25 multiplier (player moves to reactive defense, not anticipatory)

### 2.4 Tactical Decision-Making Under Pressure

**This is where fatigue causes the most visible breakdown.**

Fresh midfielder (minute 20): Pressed by opponent, quickly identifies 3 passing options, executes a safe pass to open player.

Fatigued midfielder (minute 78): Pressed by opponent, can only identify 1-2 options (tunnel vision), takes longer to execute, often plays unsafe pass (forcing it to the wrong target).

**Why?** Under-pressure decisions require:
1. Quickly identifying self as "in danger"
2. Rapidly expanding field of awareness (NOT reducing it)
3. Quickly evaluating multiple options
4. Executing with precision under time pressure

Fatigue reduces the brain's ability to EXPAND awareness when needed, leading to tunnel vision.

**Coach insight — Ancelotti (Real Madrid, Bayern, Everton):**
> "A player's quality is visible in possession. Their intelligence is visible when pressed. When fatigued, even intelligent players lose their intelligence because they can't process fast enough. This is why I train pressing resistance — not by running more, but by making 20-minute decision-making drills inside a smaller space where fatigue compounds faster."

Ancelotti's approach is deliberate: Train decision-making in conditions MORE fatiguing than match conditions, so that actual match fatigue feels manageable.

**Decision-making breakdown timeline under pressure:**
- Minutes 0-45: Players expand awareness when pressed, find quality passes (6-7 options typically available in brain)
- Minutes 45-70: Options reduce slightly (4-5 available), execution speed drops
- Minutes 70-85: Options reduce significantly (2-3 available), tunnel vision begins
- Minutes 85-90: Severe tunnel vision, risky passes increase, turnovers increase

**Statistical evidence:** Carling et al. (Journal of Sports Sciences, 2016) analyzed elite French Ligue 1 matches and found:
- Pass accuracy when pressed in first half: 82%
- Pass accuracy when pressed in final 15 minutes: 71% (-11 percentage points)
- Dangerous passes (risky, likely to turn over) in first half: 8%
- Dangerous passes in final 15 minutes: 18% (+10 percentage points)

## Part 3: Age Group & Position-Specific Fatigue Effects

### 3.1 Youth Development Fatigue Curves (U10-U18)

**Critical insight:** Fatigue degradation is NOT the same across age groups. This is because:
1. Aerobic capacity improves with age
2. Work capacity increases (body can tolerate higher metabolic load)
3. Decision-making speed improves with maturation
4. Attention resilience improves (older brains maintain focus longer)

**U10-U12 (Foundation Stage)**
- Match duration: 40-50 minutes
- Fatigue onset: Around minute 25-30
- Decision-making degradation: -15 to -20% from minute 25 onward
- Typical problem: Children's attention collapses toward end of first half

**Why?** Young children have not yet developed robust sustained attention. The basal ganglia (critical for procedural decision-making in soccer) is still maturing. Children's glycogen stores are smaller relative to body size.

**Coaching implication:** Training should include frequent breaks (every 15-20 minutes) and should not emphasize end-of-match fatigue resistance. Instead, train decision-making in FRESH states to build strong neural patterns.

**U12-U15 (Development Stage)**
- Match duration: 60-70 minutes
- Fatigue onset: Around minute 35-40
- Decision-making degradation: -15 to -20% from minute 40, worsening to -25 to -30% from minute 60
- Typical problem: Second-half collapses in decision-making, though physical capacity looks fine

**Why?** Adolescent brains are developing frontal lobe capacity (executive function, decision-making speed) but haven't reached adult levels. The mismatch between physical development (relatively mature by U14-15) and cognitive development (still maturing at 17-18) creates a vulnerability: Players look physically capable but make poor decisions.

**Coaching implication:** This age group benefits from "decision-making under fatigue" training. Short bursts of high-intensity work followed by positioning/decision-making drills train the neural adaptation to maintain decision-making when fatigued.

**U15-U18 (Maturation Stage)**
- Match duration: 80-90 minutes  
- Fatigue onset: Around minute 50-60
- Decision-making degradation: -15 to -20% from minute 60, worsening to -25 to -35% from minute 75
- Typical problem: Final 15-minute collapses in creative decisions, but defensive decisions remain stable (pattern recognition is automatic)

**Why?** By U16-17, cognitive systems are approaching adult levels. However, work capacity (the ability to maintain intensity for 90 minutes) is not yet fully developed. The fatigue curve follows elite patterns but with slightly higher degradation due to lower work capacity.

**Coaching implication:** U16+ players benefit from full-match conditioning where they learn to manage intensity across 90 minutes rather than simply trying to "be fresh."

**U18+ (Elite/Professional)**
- Match duration: 90 minutes
- Fatigue onset: Around minute 50-65
- Decision-making degradation: -15 to -20% from minute 65, worsening to -25 to -35% from minute 80
- Typical problem: Precision in creative decisions, not availability of decisions

**Why?** Elite-developed players have high work capacity and strong decision-making neural pathways. Fatigue doesn't reduce their ability to THINK, but it reduces their ability to EXECUTE with precision.

### 3.2 Position-Specific Fatigue Effects

**Different positions fatigue differently because they have different work demands.**

#### Defenders (CB, FB, LB, RB)

**First-half fatigue pattern:**
- High physical demands (tracking opposition movement, sprinting)
- High tactical demands (reading play, positioning)
- High psychological stress (one mistake = goal)

**Second-half fatigue pattern:**
- Physical fatigue is visible (slower sprints)
- Tactical fatigue is DEVASTATING (poor positioning, misjudged distances)
- Psychological fatigue compounds (more mistakes = more stress = worse decisions)

**Scanning degradation impact:** Most critical. Defenders depend on scanning for "second-wave" attacking threats. As fatigue reduces scanning frequency, defenders:
- Don't see dangerous runs developing
- React late to overlapping fullbacks
- Create space for trailing midfielders

**Coach insight — Dyche (Everton, Burnley):**
> "Defending is a decision every three seconds for 90 minutes. That's 1,800 decisions. A defender who makes perfect decisions for 70 minutes then collapses in the final 20 is useless. We train decision-making for 95 minutes because the mental fatigue of 1,800 decisions is the enemy."

Dyche's point: Defensive decision-making fatigue is CUMULATIVE. Each decision costs attention. By minute 85, defenders are running on fumes, not from physical fatigue but from cognitive fatigue.

**Defensive degradation timeline:**
- Minutes 0-50: Peak defensive decision-making
- Minutes 50-70: -10 to -15% accuracy (but not visibly bad)
- Minutes 70-85: -25 to -35% accuracy (visible mistakes, positioning errors)
- Minutes 85-90: -35 to -45% accuracy (tactical chaos, individual actions only)

#### Midfielders (CDM, CM, CAM)

**First-half fatigue pattern:**
- Highest distance covered of any position
- Highest number of decision points (possession and pressing)
- Significant psychological stress (expected to control the game)

**Second-half fatigue pattern:**
- Physical fatigue is VISIBLE (reduced distance covered, slower positioning)
- Tactical fatigue is SEVERE (reduced decision-making options, poor pressing timing)
- Psychological fatigue is CRITICAL (confidence drops, begins avoiding responsibility)

**Scanning degradation impact:** Catastrophic. Midfielders are the "eyes" of the team. When their scanning degrades:
- Pressing becomes poorly timed (players don't see passing lanes opening)
- Distribution becomes inaccurate (don't see passing options in time)
- Defensive coverage breaks down (don't see forward runs)

**Research:** Di Salvo et al. (Journal of Sports Sciences, 2013) analyzed elite midfielder running patterns:
- High-intensity running in minutes 0-15: 85-90% of peak
- High-intensity running in minutes 45-60: 75-80% of peak (25% reduction by second half)
- High-intensity running in minutes 75-90: 50-60% of peak (40-50% reduction by end)

But decision-making demands stay the same or INCREASE (more critical moments in final 15 minutes).

**Midfielder degradation timeline:**
- Minutes 0-50: Peak pressing, distribution, positioning
- Minutes 50-70: -15 to -20% decision-making speed (slower to commit to press, slower to identify passing lanes)
- Minutes 70-85: -25 to -35% decision-making quality (pressing becomes risky, distribution becomes sideways/backward, off-ball positioning deteriorates)
- Minutes 85-90: -40 to -50% decision-making quality (essentially reactive play)

#### Forwards (ST, LW, RW, CAM)

**First-half fatigue pattern:**
- Moderate distance covered (less than midfielders/defenders)
- High decision-making demands (constant positioning adjustments)
- High psychological stress (directly responsible for goal-scoring opportunities)

**Second-half fatigue pattern:**
- Physical fatigue is LESS VISIBLE (forwards don't run as much anyway)
- Tactical fatigue is DEVASTATING (positioning becomes poor, decision-making in final 3rd deteriorates)
- Psychological fatigue is VARIABLE (some forwards thrive on pressure, others collapse mentally)

**Scanning degradation impact:** CRITICAL in specific moments. Forwards don't scan constantly like midfielders, but when they DO scan (during build-up, before pressing), fatigue reduces the quality of that scan significantly.

**Decision-making degradation specifics:** Forwards degrade differently:
- **Hold-up play decisions:** Little degradation (use similar amount of attention whether fresh or tired)
- **Final 3rd positioning:** -25 to -35% degradation (poor angles, timing, positioning)
- **Pressing timing:** -30 to -40% degradation (commit late, poor recognition of opportunity)
- **Finishing:** -20 to -30% degradation (composition errors more than technical errors; trying fancy finishing when simple is better)

**Coach insight — Ten Hag (Ajax, Manchester United):**
> "A striker in the 85th minute is either winning because of positioning or losing because of poor positioning. Physical is secondary. We measure positioning accuracy every 10 seconds and train strikers to position BETTER when tired, not to run faster when fresh."

Ten Hag's insight points to position-specific fatigue management: Strikers should focus on decision-making (positioning, timing) rather than physical (running, sprinting) because that's where fatigue hits them hardest.

## Part 4: Training & Coaching Fatigue Resilience

### 4.1 Elite Coach Philosophies on Conditioning

#### Guardiola's Approach: "Decision-Making Under Load"

Manchester City trains decision-making explicitly in fatigued states:

**Session structure example:**
1. Warm-up (10 min)
2. Positional rondo (8v4 in 30x40m, 20 min) — relatively fresh
3. High-intensity interval training (6 x 4 min @ 95% max heart rate, 2 min recovery, 30 min total)
4. IMMEDIATELY after HTI: Positional possession game (11v11 in half-field, 15 min)
   - **This is where decision-making is scored**
   - Players are fatigued (heart rate 160-180 bpm)
   - Decisions made here are what matters in minute 75 of a match

**Philosophy:** "Conditioning is not running capacity. It's decision-making under high metabolic and mental load. If you train decisions fresh, you collapse when fatigued."

**Result:** Manchester City is famous for scoring 80+ goals per season while maintaining high pressing intensity in final 15 minutes. This is not luck — it's deliberate training of decision-making under fatigue.

#### Ancelotti's Approach: "Intention-Based Conditioning"

Real Madrid trains fatigue management through intentional, structured plays rather than generic "conditioning" circuits:

**Session structure example:**
1. Warm-up (10 min)
2. Position-specific skill work in small groups (20 min) — fresh state
3. Pressing game (11v8, pressing team must win ball in opponent half, 15 min)
4. Transition game (counter-attacks, 3-4 attacks per side, rest is defensive shape work, 20 min) — **critical moment**
   - **Defenders are learning to position correctly after high-intensity defending**
   - **Attackers are learning to finish after intensive defending**

**Philosophy:** "Conditioning is context-specific. We don't run in circles. We press, defend, counter, attack — the same 90-minute rhythm. Fatigue in this context is match-specific fatigue."

**Result:** Real Madrid often IMPROVES in intensity after minute 70, switching to counter-attacking football that exploits opposition fatigue. This is because defenders and forwards are trained to execute tactical decisions under fatigue.

#### Potter's Approach: "Attention Management Through Pressing Cycles"

Brighton trains attention resilience through carefully managed pressing intensity:

**Session structure example:**
1. Warm-up (10 min)
2. Rondo (12 min) — calm, decision-making focused
3. Pressing circuit (4 phases, 3 min each):
   - **Phase 1 (3 min):** High-intensity press (80+ recoveries per 3 min)
   - **Phase 2 (3 min):** Recovery press (40-50 recoveries per 3 min)
   - **Phase 3 (3 min):** Selective press (15-20 recoveries per 3 min)
   - **Phase 4 (3 min):** Recovery press (40-50 recoveries per 3 min)
4. Full-field game (20 min) — players execute pressing according to time and score

**Philosophy:** "Attention is finite. We don't train constant pressing. We train CYCLING through different intensities. This teaches players to preserve attention for critical moments."

**Result:** Brighton is famous for maintaining high-quality defensive decisions in final 15 minutes despite having less physical capacity than larger clubs. This is because they don't waste attention early in matches.

### 4.2 Age-Specific Conditioning Approaches

#### U10-U12: Avoid Fatigue, Optimize Decision-Making Quality

**Training principle:** Do NOT train fatigue resistance. Train decision-making in fresh states.

**Session structure:**
- Short practices (30-40 min for U10, 45-50 min for U11-12)
- Frequent breaks every 15 min
- No conditioning circuits; all decisions made in fresh state
- Focus: building strong neural pathways for decision-making

**Why:** Young players need to build robust decision-making schemas. If trained under fatigue, they'll develop poor techniques and poor decision-making patterns.

#### U12-U15: Introduce Decision-Making Under Fatigue

**Training principle:** Train decision-making explicitly in fatigued states, but preserve match freshness.

**Session structure:**
- High-intensity circuits (4-6 x 3-4 min) FOLLOWED BY decision-making drills (passing, positioning, small-sided games)
- This teaches brain to make good decisions despite fatigue
- Frequency: 1-2x per week

**Why:** U12-15 players are developing executive function rapidly. Training decisions under fatigue accelerates this development without damaging decision-making schemas.

#### U15-U18: Match-Specific Conditioning

**Training principle:** Train fatigue conditions matching 90-minute match rhythm.

**Session structure:**
- Full-match simulation: 90 minutes of match-intensity play
- Recovery: 15 min rest (half-time)
- Critical focus: Decisions in minutes 65-75 and 80-90
- Scoring: Decisions in final 15 minutes scored with same weight as decisions in first 15 minutes

**Why:** Elite development requires learning the actual fatigue curve of 90-minute matches. Players must experience, recover, and adapt across multiple fatigued windows.

## Part 5: Implications for Leo's Scoring System

### 5.1 Fatigue Multiplier Framework

Based on research and coaching philosophy, Leo should implement **position-specific, phase-specific fatigue multipliers:**

#### Defender Fatigue Multiplier (CB, LB, RB)

```
Minutes 0-50: x1.0 (no multiplier)
Minutes 50-70: x0.85 (15% degradation expected)
Minutes 70-85: x0.65-0.70 (30-35% degradation expected)
Minutes 85-90: x0.55-0.60 (40-45% degradation expected)
```

**Application example:**
- Defender makes a well-positioned interception in minute 25 = Score as observed (8/10)
- Defender makes the same quality interception in minute 78 = Apply 0.65 multiplier = 5.2/10 (reflects that fatigue made this harder)
- If defender makes a POOR positioning decision in minute 78 (scores 4/10 normally) = Apply 0.65 multiplier = 2.6/10 (severe penalty for fatigue-impaired decision-making)

**Problem with direct multiplier:** This penalizes tired defenders. **Better approach:**

**Revised fatigue score for defenders:**
- Score what you see (execution quality)
- ADJUST EXPECTATION based on fatigue window
- **Execution quality (8/10) in minute 78 = "Very good under fatigue" instead of "Good"**
- This uses scoring language to account for fatigue rather than multipliers

#### Midfielder Fatigue Multiplier (CDM, CM, CAM)

```
Minutes 0-50: x1.0 (no multiplier)
Minutes 50-70: x0.80-0.85 (15-20% degradation)
Minutes 70-85: x0.65-0.75 (25-35% degradation)
Minutes 85-90: x0.50-0.65 (35-50% degradation)
```

Midfielders degrade faster because they have higher baseline scanning/decision-making demands.

#### Forward Fatigue Multiplier (ST, LW, RW)

```
Minutes 0-60: x1.0 (no multiplier)
Minutes 60-75: x0.85-0.90 (10-15% degradation)
Minutes 75-85: x0.70-0.80 (20-30% degradation)
Minutes 85-90: x0.60-0.75 (25-40% degradation)
```

Forwards degrade slower in decision-making because they have lower baseline demands. BUT execution (finishing, movement precision) degrades faster.

### 5.2 Decision-Making Category Degradation Rates

Not all decision-making degrades equally:

**Slow degradation (most resilient to fatigue):**
- Basic passing (sideways, backward)
- Defensive positioning (deep)
- Ball-winning decisions

**Medium degradation:**
- Creative passing (forward, risky angles)
- Off-ball positioning (attacking)
- Pressing timing

**Fast degradation (most vulnerable to fatigue):**
- Scanning frequency
- Anticipatory positioning
- Quick decision-making under pressure
- Finishing (composition, technique)
- Pressing aggressive runs

**Scoring implication:** When assessing decision-making in minutes 70+:
- Reward safe, structured decisions (sideways passes, defensive positions)
- Penalize risky decisions more heavily (forcing it through tight spaces, aggressive pressing)
- Expect degradation in scan-dependent awareness
- Expect better play from "simple" decisions than from "creative" decisions

## Part 6: What Coaches Still Disagree On

### 6.1 The Constant Pressing Debate

**Position A (Klopp, Rangnick, Guardiola):** Teams should press with structured intensity cycles, not constant pressure.

**Position B (Ten Hag, Tuchel):** Teams should press CONSTANTS but train players' neural adaptation to handle fatigue, not back off.

**The contradiction:** Both approaches work at elite levels. Liverpool (Klopp) and Manchester United (Ten Hag) both maintain elite defensive records despite different philosophies.

**Likely explanation:** The difference is in training methods, not in pressing philosophy itself.
- Klopp: Trains pressing intensity cycles, expects players to press 80% in minute 75
- Ten Hag: Trains decision-making under fatigue, expects players to press 100% but more selectively in minute 75

Same tactical outcome (controlled pressing late), different methodology.

### 6.2 The Position-Specific Fatigue Debate

**Position A:** Defenders fatigue faster because they have reactive demands, not proactive.

**Position B:** Forwards fatigue faster because they have lower match intensity baseline, so fatigue is more noticeable.

**Position C:** Everyone fatigues similarly, but fatigue manifests differently by position.

**Likely explanation:** All three are partially true. Research shows:
- Defenders cover more ground, so physical fatigue is severe
- Forwards make fewer touches, so decision-making fatigue is severe
- Midfielders fatigue most comprehensively (both physical and cognitive)

## Part 7: Outstanding Questions & Research Gaps

### Questions about the Fatigue-Decision-Making Link

1. **Does scanning frequency CAUSE decision-making degradation, or is it a symptom of cognitive fatigue?** 
   - If a player scans less in minute 78, is that BECAUSE they're fatigued, or are they choosing to conserve attention?
   - Research implication: Track whether reduced scanning correlates with poor decisions, or whether some players intentionally reduce scanning to focus on execution.

2. **Is there a "fatigue sweet spot" where players make BETTER decisions due to reduced overthinking?**
   - Novice players often improve under pressure (paralysis breaks). Do elite players ever improve under fatigue through this mechanism?

## Conclusion

Fatigue is not the enemy of decision-making — **poor preparation for fatigue** is the enemy.

Elite coaches like Guardiola, Ancelotti, and Potter don't try to avoid fatigue. They design training that explicitly practices decision-making under fatigue. They manage fatigue tactically (pressing intensity cycles, formation adjustments, substitution timing). They build players whose decision-making architecture is ROBUST to fatigue.

For Leo's scoring system, the key implication is: **Fatigue is a context modifier, not a penalty.** A fatigued player making a reasonable decision should be scored as "good under fatigue" rather than "mediocre." Conversely, a poor decision under fatigue should be penalized more heavily because fatigue constraints were already accounted for.

The fatigue curve across 90 minutes is predictable, position-specific, and coach-able. With proper training and tactical management, the final 15 minutes don't have to be a collapse — they can be where elite teams separate from the rest.
