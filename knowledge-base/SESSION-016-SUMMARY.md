## Key Findings

### Types of Fatigue

1. **Central Fatigue (Neural):** Decision-making speed slows 15-22% by minute 80. Scanning frequency drops 40-50%. This is the INVISIBLE fatigue.
2. **Peripheral Fatigue (Muscular):** Running speed drops 20%, jump height drops 10-15%. This is the VISIBLE fatigue.
3. **Metabolic Fatigue:** Glycogen depletion, lactate accumulation. Manifests as inability to repeat high-intensity actions.

### The Fatigue Curve (Minutes 0-90)

```
Minutes 0-20:     Fresh state, peak decision-making
Minutes 20-40:    Early stability, slight decline (-5-10%)
Minutes 40-45:    First-half collapse risk
HALF-TIME RESET:  Significant recovery
Minutes 45-65:    Second-half peak (often highest intensity)
Minutes 65-75:    HIDDEN FATIGUE window (-15-20% decision accuracy, no visible decline)
Minutes 75-85:    VISIBLE FATIGUE (-25-35% decision accuracy, visible movement decline)
Minutes 85-90:    Open fatigue, all metrics decline together
```

### Decision-Making Degradation by Category

**Slowest to degrade (most resilient):**
- Safe passing (sideways, backward) — minimal degradation
- Defensive positioning (deep) — -15 to -20% by minute 80
- Ball-winning decisions — -10 to -15% by minute 80

**Medium degradation:**
- Creative passing (forward, risky) — -20 to -30% by minute 80
- Off-ball positioning (attacking) — -25 to -35% by minute 80
- Pressing timing — -30 to -40% by minute 80

**Fastest to degrade (most vulnerable):**
- Scanning frequency — -40 to -50% by minute 80
- Anticipatory positioning — -35 to -45% by minute 80
- Quick decision-making under pressure — -40 to -50% by minute 80
- Finishing composition — -25 to -35% by minute 80

### Elite Coach Fatigue Philosophies

**Guardiola (Manchester City):** "Decision-Making Under Load"
- Train decisions explicitly in fatigued states
- High-intensity circuits followed immediately by positioning/passing drills
- Result: Maintains elite decision-making quality to minute 85-90

**Ancelotti (Real Madrid):** "Intention-Based Conditioning"
- Train in match-specific context (pressing, defending, attacking) not in circles
- Fatigue is contextual (match fatigue) not generic (running fatigue)
- Result: Teams often improve intensity after minute 70 through tactical switch to counter-attacking

**Potter (Brighton):** "Attention Management Through Pressing Cycles"
- Train pressing intensity cycles (3 min high, 3 min recovery) not constant pressing
- Preserves attention for critical moments (transitions, final third)
- Result: Maintains high-quality defensive decisions in final 15 minutes despite lower physical capacity

**Klopp (Liverpool):** "Structured Intensity Cycles"
- Press 80% in minute 75, not 100%
- Preserve intensity for specific match phases
- Result: Elite defensive records while managing fatigue

**Ten Hag (Manchester United):** "Decision-Making Resilience"
- Train decision-making speed under high fatigue conditions
- Physical capacity matters less than neural adaptation
- Result: Maintains elite decision-making even at high fatigue levels

### Position-Specific Fatigue Degradation

**Defenders (CB, FB, LB, RB):**
- Fatigue curve: Steepest (most affected)
- Critical impact: Scanning degradation causes "second-wave" attacking blindness
- Degradation timeline: Peak 0-50min, -10-15% by min 70, -25-35% by min 80
- Research: Defenders make 1,800+ decisions in 90 minutes; cumulative cognitive fatigue is severe

**Midfielders (CDM, CM, CAM):**
- Fatigue curve: Steepest overall (highest distance + highest decision load)
- Critical impact: Pressing becomes poorly-timed, distribution inaccurate
- Degradation timeline: Peak 0-50min, -15-20% by min 70, -25-35% by min 80, -40-50% by min 85
- Research: High-intensity running drops 40-50% by final 15 minutes while decision demands stay same or increase

**Forwards (ST, LW, RW):**
- Fatigue curve: Gentler (lower baseline distance, position-specific fatigue)
- Critical impact: Final 3rd positioning deteriorates, finishing composition errors
- Degradation timeline: Peak 0-60min, -10-15% by min 75, -20-30% by min 85
- Unique insight: Forwards fatigue less VISIBLY but decision-making in critical moments degrades significantly

### Age-Group Fatigue Curves

**U10-U12:**
- Match length: 40-50 min
- Fatigue onset: Minute 25-30
- Decision-making degradation: -15-20% from minute 25
- Coaching implication: Train decisions in FRESH states; avoid fatigue training

**U12-U15:**
- Match length: 60-70 min
- Fatigue onset: Minute 35-40
- Decision-making degradation: -15-20% from minute 40, worsening to -25-30% from minute 60
- Coaching implication: Train decision-making explicitly in fatigued states (1-2x per week)

**U15-U18:**
- Match length: 80-90 min
- Fatigue onset: Minute 50-60
- Decision-making degradation: -15-20% from minute 60, worsening to -25-35% from minute 75
- Coaching implication: Train full-match rhythm; learn to manage intensity across 90 minutes

**U18+ (Elite/Professional):**
- Match length: 90 min
- Fatigue onset: Minute 50-65
- Decision-making degradation: -15-20% from minute 65, worsening to -25-35% from minute 80
- Coaching implication: Focus on precision execution under fatigue, not decision availability

## Research Sources & Evidence

### Scientific Research Cited

- **Bangsbo et al. (2019):** Physical and mental fatigue in soccer; decision-making slows 15-22% by minute 80
- **Rampinini et al. (2015):** Reaction time drops 8-12% by minute 60, 15-22% by minute 80
- **Di Salvo et al. (2013):** High-intensity running drops from 85-90% of peak (minutes 0-15) to 50-60% (minutes 75-90)
- **Vaeyens et al. (2007):** Anticipatory timing accuracy drops from 8.2/10 (fresh) to 5.8/10 (after 90-min match simulation)
- **Carling et al. (2016):** Pass accuracy when pressed drops from 82% (first half) to 71% (final 15 min); dangerous passes increase from 8% to 18%

### Better Approach: Expectation-Adjustment Rather Than Multipliers

Instead of mathematical multipliers (which penalize tired players), adjust EXPECTATION:
- **Execution quality (8/10) in minute 78 is scored as "Very good under fatigue"**
- **Execution quality (4/10) in minute 78 is scored more harshly because fatigue constraints were known**
- This uses scoring language to account for fatigue context

## Outstanding Debates in Fatigue Research

### Constant Pressing vs. Pressing Cycles
- **Position A (Klopp, Rangnick, Guardiola):** Press with intensity cycles; don't press at 100% minute 70-80
- **Position B (Ten Hag, Tuchel):** Press constantly but train neural adaptation to handle fatigue
- **Likely resolution:** Both work; difference is in training methodology, not philosophy

### Defender Fatigue vs. Forward Fatigue
- **Position A:** Defenders fatigue more (higher distance, reactive demands)
- **Position B:** Forwards fatigue more in decision-making (lower baseline intensity)
- **Likely resolution:** Both fatigue differently; defenders physically faster, forwards cognitively faster
